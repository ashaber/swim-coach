## 1. Executive summary

- **The core model is simple and the arithmetic is not controversial.**
  Energy availability (EA) = dietary energy intake minus exercise energy
  expenditure, normalised to fat-free mass (kcal/kg FFM/day). What is left
  over is what the body has to run everything that isn't exercise. If EA is
  chronically too low, physiology down-regulates. This much is agreed by
  both the IOC consensus authors and their sharpest critics
  (`Mountjoy et al. 2023`; `Jeukendrup et al. 2024`). Confidence: high.

- **The famous "30 kcal/kg FFM/day" threshold is NOT a settled clinical
  number and should never be presented to the athlete as one.** It comes
  from short (5-day) laboratory studies in *sedentary, young, regularly
  menstruating women* (`Loucks & Thuma 2003`), where LH pulsatility was
  disrupted below ~30 kcal/kg LBM/day. `Areta, Taylor & Koehler (2021)`
  state plainly that the existence of specific EA thresholds "is debated in
  females and unknown in males." **The 2023 IOC consensus itself abandoned
  fixed cut-off values** in favour of a spectrum ("adaptable" vs
  "problematic" LEA) plus a primary/secondary indicator model
  (`Mountjoy et al. 2023`). Confidence: high that the number is contested;
  the number itself is *not* a defensible engine constant.

- **EA cannot be measured accurately enough in the field to diagnose
  anything.** This is the single most important practical finding in the
  whole pass. `Burke, Lundy, Fahrenholtz & Melin (2018)` catalogue the
  measurement pitfalls (no universal protocol, error in every term of the
  equation, no consensus on what "low" means). `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)`
  go further: food-diary under-reporting up to 60%, FFM measurement error,
  ambiguity about what counts as "exercise" energy expenditure — and cite a
  worked example where a study's reported 34% LEA prevalence collapsed to 5%
  once measurement error was corrected. **A coaching app must not compute an
  EA number and act on it.** Confidence: high.

- **The syndrome's evidence base is genuinely contested, and this must be
  surfaced honestly rather than smoothed.** Three separate critical
  currents: (1) the Female Athlete Triad group's formal refutation of the
  original RED-S consensus (`De Souza, Williams, Nattiv et al. 2014`);
  (2) the "the prospective evidence is thin" critique — ~90% of the
  empirical literature is observational, controlled LEA interventions
  typically last ≤5 days (`Areta et al. 2021`; `Jeukendrup et al. 2024`);
  (3) the circularity critique — REDs is defined as symptoms *caused by*
  LEA, but LEA can't be measured, so diagnosis rests on the same generic
  symptoms, which have many other causes (`Jeukendrup et al. 2024`, who
  propose an alternative multi-cause checklist rather than a single
  energy-deficit explanation). Confidence: high that the dispute is real and
  ongoing. **This does not mean LEA is fake** — it means the *diagnostic
  confidence* claimed for it in popular sports media outruns the data.

- **What IS well-supported: the downstream *outcomes* are real and serious
  once they appear.** Menstrual dysfunction (in menstruating athletes) is
  associated with ~4.5× the bone-injury rate in elite distance athletes
  (`Heikura et al. 2018`). Secondary amenorrhea in physically active women
  is associated with impaired endothelial function and worse lipids —
  i.e. it can *cancel out* the cardiovascular benefit of the exercise
  (`Tegg et al. 2024`, meta-analysis). Confidence: medium-high (associations
  from observational data, but consistent and large).

- **The strongest causal performance evidence is recent, in women, and
  directly relevant to the ~90-min wall.** `Caldwell et al. (2024)`:
  12 endurance-trained women, randomised blinded crossover, 14 days at
  LEA (22.3 kcal/kg FFM/day) vs optimal EA (51.9). LEA impaired 20-min
  time-trial performance by **7.8%** — and the impairment **persisted after
  3 days of refuelling**. Notably, muscle glycogen and mitochondrial
  capacity were *unchanged*: the performance loss was not a simple
  glycogen-depletion story. Confidence: medium-high (n=12, 14 days, not
  swimmers, not masters — but it is a controlled crossover in trained
  females, which is rare).
  **Direct implication for Renee's question:** chronic LEA is a *plausible*
  upstream contributor to poor session durability, and — importantly —
  a well-executed single-session fuelling fix would NOT be expected to
  resolve it. If better in-session fuelling (the `08-ultra-feeding.md` pass)
  does not move the 90-min wall within a few weeks, chronic EA becomes a
  more likely explanation and is a reason to see a sports physician /
  sports dietitian, not to add training.

- **There is exactly one genuinely swim-specific performance study, and it
  is striking.** `VanHeest et al. (2014)`: 10 junior elite female swimmers
  over 12 weeks; the ovarian-suppressed group *lost* 9.8% of 400 m swim
  velocity while the normally-cycling group *gained* 8.2%, with biochemical
  evidence of energy conservation (suppressed T3). Confidence: high for
  population match (swimmers), low-medium for transfer to Renee (n=10,
  ages 15-17, pool sprint/middle-distance, and the mechanism runs through
  ovarian suppression, which is not an available signal for a peri-/
  post-menopausal athlete). `[EVIDENCE: swim]` — but read the caveat.

- **Swimming is non-weight-bearing, and this is the most under-appreciated
  item in the whole dossier.** Two independent systematic reviews from the
  same group: `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2013)` (64 studies) and
  `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2018)` (meta-analysis, 18-30y) both find swimmers'
  BMD is **similar to sedentary controls and lower than high-impact
  athletes** — i.e. swimming does *not* buy the bone protection that
  running/jumping does, even in well-fuelled swimmers. So for a swimmer,
  "my training is protecting my bones" is false, and LEA's bone cost lands
  on a skeleton that has no sport-derived buffer. `Hutson et al. (2021)`
  argue high-impact loading is an under-used, energy-cheap countermeasure
  for exactly this population. Confidence: high for the BMD finding,
  medium for the countermeasure (narrative review, proposal not trial).
  **This is the single most actionable, swim-specific item here** — and it
  points at dryland/impact work, not at a fuelling rule.

- **The masters / peri-menopausal picture is close to an evidence vacuum,
  and the classic warning sign is unavailable.** The two big prevalence/
  outcome studies most often cited are in athletes aged 15-30
  (`Ackerman et al. 2019`, 1000 female athletes 15-30y, entirely
  self-report) and 18-40 (`Henninger et al. 2024`, trail/ultra runners).
  For an athlete past menopause, **amenorrhea — the syndrome's canonical
  red flag — carries no information**, and the screening instrument built
  around it inherits that problem. `Gowers et al. (2025)` is the closest
  thing found to a masters-aged endurance cohort (females 45±13y): 77% of
  their female athletes flagged "at risk" on LEAF-Q, and **the authors
  themselves warn that peri-menopausal symptoms may have artificially
  inflated those scores**. Confidence: high that this is a real gap; the
  honest answer for Renee's demographic is "we largely don't know."

- **Screening tools: useful for ruling OUT, unreliable for ruling IN — and
  the one swim study is the worst case.** `Rogers et al. (2021)`: LEAF-Q has
  high sensitivity/negative-predictive value but **low specificity** — it
  cannot serve as a diagnostic surrogate. `Klein et al. (2023)` — the only
  swimmer-specific LEA study found — reports 43% of NCAA D-III swimmers
  below 30 kcal/kg FFM, and found **LEAF-Q did not discriminate** low-EA
  from adequate-EA female swimmers (caught 3 of 6, false-flagged 3 others).
  `[EVIDENCE: swim]` for the swimmer finding. Confidence: medium-high.

- **RMR ratio (measured vs predicted resting metabolic rate) is a popular
  surrogate whose support is eroding.** `Sterringer & Larson-Meyer (2022)`
  call it promising but unstandardised; `Espinar et al. (2025)` found **no
  association at all** between EA and RMR or RMR-ratio in 49 competitive
  athletes. Do not treat RMR ratio as a settled test. Confidence: medium.

- **Cold water and appetite: the folklore is half-right, and it cuts the
  opposite way from what most people assume.** Water-based exercise does
  **not** suppress appetite relative to land-based exercise, and it
  *increases* post-exercise intake vs rest; exercising in **cold water
  (18-20 °C) increases subsequent energy intake substantially more than
  neutral water** (`Grigg et al. 2023`, meta-analysis of 8 acute studies;
  the classic underlying study is `White et al. 2005`, n=11 men). So the
  swim-specific LEA hazard is **not** an appetite-suppression mechanism —
  it is (a) very large exercise energy expenditure, (b) long sessions during
  which eating is mechanically hard, and (c) a cold stimulus that raises
  the bill further. Confidence: medium (small acute-lab studies, mostly men,
  cycle ergometers in water rather than swimming). Practical read: post-swim
  hunger is a *legitimate physiological signal*, not something to override.

- **What helps is unglamorous and mostly not a coaching decision:** raise
  energy intake (the one intervention study found `+360 kcal/day` for 6
  months restored menses in 7 of 8 exercise-associated amenorrheic athletes
  — `Cialdella-Kam et al. 2014`), spread energy across the day rather than
  back-loading it (`Fahrenholtz et al. 2018`), reduce exercise energy
  expenditure where possible, and add bone-loading impact work
  (`Hutson et al. 2021`). Note what the Cialdella-Kam study did *not* find:
  bone and muscle outcomes barely moved in 6 months. **Bone damage is slow
  to accrue and slow to reverse.** Confidence: medium (n=8 intervention).

- **Net position for this system:** treat LEA as a *risk pattern to flag and
  escalate*, never as a diagnosis to make. The only defensible engine-side
  moves are (a) a fuelling-adequacy prompt tied to very large training days,
  (b) a persistent-pattern flag (durability loss + poor recovery + weight
  loss + illness/injury clustering) that recommends **clinical evaluation**,
  and (c) a bone-loading recommendation that is swim-specific and does not
  depend on resolving the RED-S debate at all.

## 2. Verified sources by topic

### Topic 1 — The model: what energy availability is, and how the consensus evolved

- **✓ Loucks A.B., Thuma J.R. (2003)** — "Luteinizing Hormone Pulsatility Is
  Disrupted at a Threshold of Energy Availability in Regularly Menstruating
  Women" — *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*, 88(1):297-311.
  **Verification:** publisher and PubMed listings; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed consistently.
  **Summary:** 29 regularly menstruating, **habitually sedentary** young
  women; energy availability clamped for **5 days** in the early follicular
  phase at 45 vs 10/20/30 kcal/kg LBM/day while expending 15 kcal/kg LBM/day
  in supervised exercise. LH pulsatility was disrupted below ~30 kcal/kg
  LBM/day. **This single 5-day study in sedentary women is the origin of the
  "30 kcal/kg FFM" number that the entire popular RED-S discourse rests on.**
  It was never a study of trained athletes, never of long exposures, and
  never of older women.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` at best — arguably not even that;
  the subjects were sedentary. Confidence: **low-medium** as a
  *clinical threshold for this athlete*; high as a demonstration that the
  reproductive axis is energy-sensitive.
  **Grounds:** explains where the number comes from; explicitly should NOT
  become an engine constant.

- **✓ Mountjoy M., Sundgot-Borgen J., Burke L., et al. (2014)** — "The IOC
  consensus statement: beyond the Female Athlete Triad — Relative Energy
  Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)" — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2014.
  **Verification:** title/authors/journal corroborated across multiple
  independent listings; page numbers not individually confirmed, so none are
  given here.
  **Summary:** The statement that renamed and broadened the Female Athlete
  Triad into a whole-athlete, both-sexes syndrome (RED-S) driven by low
  energy availability, with claimed effects on metabolic rate, menstrual
  function, bone health, immunity, protein synthesis and cardiovascular
  health.
  **Tag:** consensus-statement opinion — **not** primary evidence. Cite it as
  "the consensus position", never as "research shows".

- **✓ Mountjoy M. et al. (2018)** — "IOC consensus statement on relative
  energy deficiency in sport (RED-S): 2018 update" — *British Journal of
  Sports Medicine*, 52(11):687-697 (co-published in *IJSNEM* 28(4)).
  **Verification:** publisher/consensus PDF and multiple listings; title,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Reaffirms and expands the 2014 model; introduces the RED-S
  Clinical Assessment Tool (RED-S CAT) traffic-light framework for
  return-to-play decisions. Still consensus opinion, not new primary
  evidence.
  **Tag:** consensus-statement opinion. Confidence: n/a (not an evidence
  claim).

- **✓ Mountjoy M., Ackerman K.E., Bailey D.M., Burke L.M., Constantini N.,
  Hackney A.C., Heikura I.A., Melin A., Pensgaard A.M., Stellingwerff T.,
  Sundgot-Borgen J.K., Torstveit M.K., Jacobsen A.U., Verhagen E.,
  Budgett R., Engebretsen L., Erdener U. (2023)** — "2023 International
  Olympic Committee's (IOC) consensus statement on Relative Energy Deficiency
  in Sport (REDs)" — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 57(17):1073-1097.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; full author list,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** The current consensus. Synthesises 170+ publications.
  Renames RED-S → REDs. Introduces (a) a distinction between **"adaptable"**
  and **"problematic"** LEA, (b) updated Health and Performance conceptual
  models plus a new Physiological Model in which individual moderating
  factors determine whether an LEA exposure produces harm, and (c) the
  **REDs CAT2** severity/risk-stratification tool. **Crucially it moves away
  from fixed EA cut-offs toward severity stratification on primary/secondary
  indicators** — i.e. the consensus itself no longer endorses "under 30 =
  RED-S".
  **Tag:** consensus-statement opinion (high-quality, expert, but opinion).
  Confidence: n/a. **Conflict-of-interest note:** IOC-convened panel; several
  authors have built substantial research programmes on the REDs construct
  — that is not misconduct, but it is a structural interest in the construct
  surviving, and the critics (below) say so.

- **✓ Stellingwerff T., Mountjoy M., McCluskey W.T., Ackerman K.E.,
  Verhagen E., Heikura I.A. (2023)** — "Review of the scientific rationale,
  development and validation of the International Olympic Committee Relative
  Energy Deficiency in Sport Clinical Assessment Tool: V.2 (IOC REDs CAT2)"
  — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2023.
  **Verification:** title/authors/journal/year corroborated across several
  independent listings and the IOC-hosted tool PDF; exact volume/pages not
  independently confirmed, so none are given.
  **Summary:** Describes REDs CAT2: a three-step process — (1) screening,
  (2) severity/risk stratification against **primary and secondary
  indicators**, (3) **physician-led** final diagnosis and treatment plan,
  with a four-level (green/yellow/orange/red) traffic-light and associated
  sport-participation guidance.
  **Tag:** consensus/clinical-tool opinion. **The load-bearing point for this
  repo: step 3 is explicitly physician-led.** The tool's own authors place
  diagnosis outside the coach's scope. This is the citation to use whenever
  the system declines to diagnose.

### Topic 2 — The honest state of the evidence (the debate)

- **✓ Areta J.L., Taylor H.L., Koehler K. (2021)** — "Low energy
  availability: history, definition and evidence of its endocrine, metabolic
  and physiological effects in prospective studies in females and males" —
  *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, 121(1):1-21.
  **Verification:** fetched full text directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Reviews the *prospective* (i.e. causal-capable) LEA
  literature and finds it small: most evidence is cross-sectional, and the
  controlled interventions "typically last ≤5 days," capturing only early
  endocrine perturbation. States the existence of specific EA thresholds "is
  debated in females and unknown in males." Flags that the EA construct
  ignores non-exercise activity thermogenesis and is error-prone in the
  field.
  **Tag:** review of primary evidence — the best single "how strong is this
  really" source found. Confidence: high in its characterisation.
  **Test:** none proposed — this is a framing source, not a numeric claim.

- **✓ Burke L.M., Lundy B., Fahrenholtz I.L., Melin A.K. (2018)** —
  "Pitfalls of Conducting and Interpreting Estimates of Energy Availability
  in Free-Living Athletes" — *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and
  Exercise Metabolism*, 28(4):350-363.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** From *inside* the RED-S research community (Burke and Melin
  are REDs consensus authors), a candid account of why field EA estimates
  are unreliable: no universal calculation protocol, substantial residual
  error in every term (intake, exercise expenditure, FFM), and no consensus
  on the "low" threshold. Recommends caution in interpreting any single EA
  number.
  **Tag:** methodological review. Confidence: high. **This is the citation
  that justifies the engine NOT computing an EA number.**

- **✓ Jeukendrup A.E., Areta J.L., Van Genechten L., et al. (2024)** —
  "Does Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs) Syndrome Exist?" —
  *Sports Medicine*, 54(11):2793-2816.
  **Verification:** fetched full text directly; title, journal, year,
  volume/pages confirmed; lead authors confirmed (full author list not fully
  enumerated in the fetched view — treat the "et al." as intentional).
  **Summary:** The most systematic published critique. Argues: (1) EA cannot
  be estimated reliably in practice (food-diary under-reporting up to 60%);
  (2) the 30 kcal/kg threshold rests on two 5-day studies in sedentary women
  and ignores inter-individual variation; (3) ~90% of the empirical REDs
  literature is observational — associations, not causation; (4) the model is
  **circular** — REDs is defined as symptoms caused only by problematic LEA,
  but since LEA can't be measured, diagnosis is made from those same generic
  symptoms, which invites confirmation bias; (5) reported LEA prevalences of
  80-100% are "possibly misleading" (one cited study's 34% fell to 5% after
  correcting for measurement error). Proposes an alternative — the **Athlete
  Health and Readiness Checklist (AHaRC)** — assessing eight independent
  stressor categories (training load, life stress, mental health, disordered
  eating, nutrition, sleep, infection, undiagnosed clinical conditions) under
  an allostatic-load framing, rather than attributing everything to calories.
  The authors state explicitly that the aim is not to debunk REDs but to
  challenge dogma.
  **Conflicts of interest:** none disclosed in the fetched text. Note for
  balance: Jeukendrup has extensive commercial sports-nutrition ties
  historically; that is worth knowing, though it cuts *against* a
  "carbohydrate/energy doesn't matter" reading, not for it.
  **Tag:** critical review. Confidence: high that the criticisms are
  well-founded; **this does not establish that LEA is harmless.**
  **Practical read for this repo:** the AHaRC framing (many stressors, not
  one) maps almost exactly onto what the engine already does with
  `wellness_composite` + training load — i.e. the system's existing
  multi-signal design is closer to the critics' recommendation than to a
  single-cause energy model.

- **✓ De Souza M.J., Williams N.I., Nattiv A., et al. (2014)** —
  "Misunderstanding the female athlete triad: refuting the IOC consensus
  statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)" —
  *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 48(20):1461-1465.
  **Verification:** title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages corroborated
  across multiple independent listings (PubMed, institutional repositories).
  **Summary:** A formal rebuttal, by the Female Athlete Triad expert panel,
  to the 2014 IOC RED-S consensus. Argues the RED-S model over-reaches
  beyond its evidence, that renaming/absorbing the Triad discards a
  well-validated, evidence-graded framework, and that the broadened
  syndrome's additional claimed consequences lacked supporting data at the
  time.
  **Tag:** expert-opinion rebuttal (not primary evidence). Confidence: n/a.
  **Why it matters here:** it establishes that the disagreement is not
  fringe — it is between two groups of senior researchers who both study
  this for a living. Any coaching text that presents RED-S as settled
  science is misrepresenting the field.

### Topic 3 — Health consequences

**Bone (the swim-specific centrepiece).**

- **✓ Gómez-Bruton A., Gónzalez-Agüero A., Gómez-Cabello A., Casajús J.A.,
  Vicente-Rodríguez G. (2013)** — "Is Bone Tissue Really Affected by
  Swimming? A Systematic Review" — *PLOS ONE*, 8(8):e70119.
  **Verification:** publisher (PLOS) and PMC listings; title, authors,
  journal, year confirmed.
  **Summary:** 64 studies of swimmers' bone mass, structure and metabolism.
  Swimmers have **lower BMD than high-impact athletes and similar BMD to
  sedentary controls**. Swimmers show higher bone turnover than controls;
  the authors note a possible structural/fracture-resistance offset and
  suggest swimming may be beneficial for bone later in life — that latter
  claim is the weakest part of the review and should not be leaned on.
  **Tag:** `[EVIDENCE: swim]`. Confidence: high for the core finding
  (swimming is not osteogenic), low for the "beneficial in later life"
  speculation.
  **Test:** not falsifiable from Renee's own logs — this is a reason to get a
  **baseline DXA**, which is a clinical referral, not a training metric.

- **✓ Gómez-Bruton A., Montero-Marín J., González-Agüero A., Gómez-Cabello A.,
  García-Campayo J., Moreno L.A., Casajús J.A., Vicente-Rodríguez G. (2018)**
  — "Swimming and peak bone mineral density: A systematic review and
  meta-analysis" — *Journal of Sports Sciences*, 36(4):365-377.
  **Verification:** publisher listing and multiple independent citation
  records; title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  (Direct PubMed fetch was blocked by a CAPTCHA; corroborated via publisher +
  aggregators instead — hence ✓, not ~.)
  **Summary:** Meta-analysis in young adults (18-30y): swimmers show BMD
  **similar to non-athletic controls** at whole-body, femoral neck and
  lumbar spine, and **lower than high-impact athletes** at the same sites.
  Confirms the 2013 review with a quantitative synthesis.
  **Tag:** `[EVIDENCE: swim]`. Confidence: high (for young adults;
  **not** studied in masters swimmers).

- **✓ Hutson M.J., O'Donnell E., Brooke-Wavell K., Sale C., Blagrove R.C.
  (2021)** — "Effects of Low Energy Availability on Bone Health in Endurance
  Athletes and High-Impact Exercise as A Potential Countermeasure: A
  Narrative Review" — *Sports Medicine*, 51(3):391-403.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Narrative review. Endurance athletes are at high risk of LEA
  and of bone stress injury. Argues **high-impact exercise is highly
  osteogenic and energy-cheap** — meaning it can improve bone without adding
  meaningfully to the energy deficit — yet is rarely used in endurance
  athletes. Proposes it as a countermeasure; explicitly a proposal, not a
  completed trial.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: medium (narrative
  review; the *osteogenic* effect of impact loading is very well established
  independently, the *LEA-countermeasure* application is a hypothesis).
  **Test:** if impact loading (e.g. brief hop/jump sets) is added to Renee's
  existing dryland strength sessions, it should be checkable that (a) it adds
  negligible session RPE/duration, and (b) it does not aggravate any joint
  complaint in her wellness log. Whether it changes BMD is **only** answerable
  by repeat DXA — i.e. clinical, not app-side.

- **✓ Heikura I.A., Uusitalo A.L.T., Stellingwerff T., Bergland D., Mero A.A.,
  Burke L.M. (2018)** — "Low Energy Availability Is Difficult to Assess but
  Outcomes Have Large Impact on Bone Injury Rates in Elite Distance Athletes"
  — *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*,
  28(4):403-411.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 59 elite distance athletes (13 amenorrheic women, 22
  eumenorrheic women, plus men split by testosterone). **Amenorrheic women
  had ~4.5× the prevalence of bone injury** vs eumenorrheic (large effect);
  low-testosterone men showed a similar ~4.5× pattern. The diagnostic-tool
  comparison is the other half of the paper: EA estimates, LEAF-Q and the
  Triad/RED-S tools **disagreed with each other**, and the authors concluded
  **current reproductive-hormone function was a more objective, accurate
  marker than dietary/training-based EA calculation**.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]` (elite distance athletes; weight-bearing
  sport — note that bone-injury rates in a *swimmer* are not comparable, but
  the *endocrine* signal is). Confidence: medium-high (cross-sectional,
  moderate n, elite).
  **Sharp caveat for Renee:** the marker this paper says is *most* reliable
  (reproductive function) is precisely the marker unavailable to a peri-/
  post-menopausal athlete.

**Cardiovascular.**

- **~ Tegg N.L., et al. (2024)** — "Impact of Secondary Amenorrhea on
  Cardiovascular Disease Risk in Physically Active Women: A Systematic Review
  and Meta-Analysis" — *Journal of the American Heart Association*, 2024.
  **Verification:** title, journal, year confirmed via publisher and PubMed
  listings; direct full-text fetch was blocked (HTTP 403), and the **full
  author list was not confirmed** (the registered protocol lists Tegg,
  Myburgh, Kennedy, Norris) — hence **~**, not ✓. Verify authorship before
  promoting to a load-bearing citation.
  **Summary (from consistent secondary/abstract sources):** Physically active
  women with secondary amenorrhea had significantly **lower estradiol, lower
  flow-mediated dilation** (endothelial dysfunction), **lower resting heart
  rate**, lower systolic and diastolic BP, and **higher total cholesterol,
  LDL and triglycerides** than eumenorrheic active women. Conclusion:
  functional hypothalamic amenorrhea may **obviate the cardiovascular
  benefits of exercise**.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (physically active women, not
  swimmers, and in menstruating-age women — **does not transfer to a
  post-menopausal athlete**, whose low estradiol has a different cause).
  Confidence: medium.
  **Why it is in this dossier anyway — the HRV/RHR confound:** this is the
  clearest verified evidence that a *harmful* energy/hormonal state can
  co-present with wearable signals a training system reads as *good*
  (lower resting HR, lower BP). See §5 cross-references.

- **~ Rickenlund A. et al. (2005)** — amenorrheic female athletes show
  reduced brachial flow-mediated dilation and worse lipid profile than
  oligomenorrheic athletes — *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology &
  Metabolism*.
  **Verification:** repeatedly and consistently described in secondary
  sources, but the primary record was **not** individually confirmed this
  pass (exact title/volume/pages unverified). **~** — do not cite until
  verified; the Tegg meta-analysis covers the same ground at review level.

**Metabolic / endocrine / within-day.**

- **✓ Fahrenholtz I.L., Sjödin A., Benardot D., Tornberg Å.B., Skouby S.,
  Faber J., Sundgot-Borgen J., Melin A.K. (2018)** — "Within-day energy
  deficiency and reproductive function in female endurance athletes" —
  *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, 28(3):1139-1146.
  **Verification:** publisher and institutional-repository listings; title,
  authors, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Re-analysis of 7-day intake/expenditure data in 25 elite
  female endurance athletes (10 eumenorrheic, 15 with menstrual
  dysfunction) with **similar 24-hour energy availability**. Athletes with
  menstrual dysfunction spent more of the day in energy deficit — i.e. the
  *timing/spread* of intake, not just the daily total, tracked with
  reproductive dysfunction, and related to RMR, T3 and cortisol.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (elite female endurance athletes,
  not swimmers; cross-sectional re-analysis, n=25). Confidence: medium.
  **Test:** for Renee, this is checkable in a soft way — on her biggest
  training days, is the bulk of intake arriving *after* the session? A
  pattern of long in-deficit windows (morning practice + delayed first real
  meal) is a flaggable habit even without computing any EA number. This is
  the most practically usable finding in the whole dossier for a
  coaching system.

**Immune / GI / psychological.** No high-quality *causal* source found for
any of these in athletes. What exists is `Ackerman et al. (2019)`
(self-reported LEA correlating with self-reported immune, GI, metabolic,
cardiovascular and psychological dysfunction — see Topic 4; a design that
cannot separate cause from shared reporting bias) and the
`Gallant et al. (2025)` meta-analysis finding LEA associated with
**increased absence from training due to illness**. Treat both as
association-level. The eating-disorder overlap is real and clinically
serious, and is the strongest reason for the "escalate to a clinician, don't
coach it" rule — it is also the reason this system must never tell an
athlete to eat less, and must be careful that anything it says about
fuelling cannot be read as a restriction cue.

### Topic 4 — Performance consequences

- **✓ Caldwell H.G., et al. (2024)** — "The whole-body and skeletal muscle
  metabolic response to 14 days of highly controlled low energy availability
  in endurance-trained females" — *The FASEB Journal*, 2024.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, lead author,
  journal, year confirmed (full author list not enumerated in the fetched
  view — "et al." is intentional).
  **Summary:** 12 endurance-trained women; **randomised, blinded crossover**;
  two 14-day arms — optimal EA (51.9 kcal/kg FFM/day) vs LEA (22.3), with
  training held at ~8 h/week. LEA: resting free fatty acids +99.4%, fat
  oxidation +28.2% at rest, glucose control/insulin sensitivity unchanged,
  **muscle glycogen and mitochondrial capacity unchanged** — and **20-min
  time-trial performance impaired 7.8%**, an impairment that **persisted
  after 3 days of refuelling**.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (endurance-trained women, not
  swimmers, not masters). Confidence: **medium-high** — small (n=12) but a
  controlled crossover, which is exactly what the field is short of, and it
  is in *women*, which most of the field is also short of.
  **Test (this is the key one for Renee's 90-min wall):** if in-session
  fuelling is corrected per `08-ultra-feeding.md` and durability at 90 min
  does **not** improve over ~3-4 weeks, that pattern is more consistent with
  a chronic-EA problem than an acute-fuelling one — and the correct next
  step is a **sports physician / registered sports dietitian**, not a
  training adjustment. The 3-day-refuel persistence in this study is exactly
  why "I ate a big dinner and still felt flat" is not evidence against LEA.

- **✓ VanHeest J.L., Rodgers C.D., Mahoney C.E., De Souza M.J. (2014)** —
  "Ovarian suppression impairs sport performance in junior elite female
  swimmers" — *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 46(1):156-166.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed and publisher listings; title,
  authors, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 10 junior elite female swimmers (15-17y) tracked over a
  12-week training cycle, classified by circulating estradiol/progesterone as
  cyclic (CYC) or ovarian-suppressed (OVS). At week 12 the OVS group showed a
  **9.8% decline in 400 m swim velocity** while CYC **improved 8.2%** — with
  suppressed total T3 in OVS indicating metabolic energy conservation.
  **Tag:** `[EVIDENCE: swim]` — the only swim-specific LEA *performance*
  study found. Confidence: **medium** overall — high for population
  (swimmers, female) but n=10, adolescents, a pool event, and observational
  (groups were classified, not randomised). **Transfer to a masters
  post-menopausal athlete is weak**, because the exposure variable
  (ovarian suppression) is not measurable in her.
  **Why cite it anyway:** it is the one piece of evidence that, in swimmers
  specifically, energy-deficiency-associated hormonal suppression tracked
  with *losing* swim speed across a training block while training was
  maintained. That is the shape of the pattern this system should be able to
  notice: **training goes in, performance goes backwards.**

- **✓ Ackerman K.E., Holtzman B., Cooper K.M., et al. (2019)** — "Low energy
  availability surrogates correlate with health and performance consequences
  of Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport" — *British Journal of Sports
  Medicine*, 53(10):628-633.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, lead authors,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 1,000 female athletes **aged 15-30**. Low EA (classified by
  **online questionnaire**) was associated with self-reported immunological,
  metabolic, psychological, cardiovascular and gastrointestinal dysfunction,
  and with reduced endurance performance, training response, judgement,
  coordination and muscle strength, plus increased irritability/depression.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: **low-medium as
  evidence, despite how often it is cited as strong.** This is
  **cross-sectional and entirely self-report on both sides of the
  correlation** — no biomarkers, no clinical assessment, no objective
  performance measure. It is the paper most often invoked to prove RED-S's
  consequences; it cannot establish causation and its exposure and outcome
  share a reporting-bias source. Also: **the cohort is 15-30 years old** —
  it says nothing about masters athletes.

- **✓ Gallant T.L., Ong L.F., Wong L., Sparks M., Wilson E., Puglisi J.L.,
  Gerriets V.A. (2025)** — "Low Energy Availability and Relative Energy
  Deficiency in Sport: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis" —
  *Sports Medicine*, 55(2):325-339 (published online 2024).
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, full author list,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 59 studies, 6,118 athletes. **44.7% of athletes across the
  literature were classified as having LEA** (44.2% of women, 49.4% of men)
  — a prevalence so high it should itself prompt suspicion about the
  classification methods (cf. `Jeukendrup et al. 2024` on over-estimation).
  Athletes with LEA showed impairments in run performance, training response,
  endurance performance, coordination, concentration, judgement, explosive
  power and agility, and higher likelihood of illness-related training
  absence. Injury findings were **mixed**, though most included studies
  reported impaired bone health / higher bone stress injury risk.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: medium — it is a
  meta-analysis, but of a literature that is overwhelmingly cross-sectional
  and self-report; garbage-in constraints apply, and the review did not
  report a formal certainty-of-evidence grading in the abstract.

### Topic 5 — Masters athletes and peri-/post-menopausal women (the gap)

**This is the section where the honest answer is mostly "not known."**

- **✓ Gowers C.R., McManus C.J., Chung H.C., Jones B., Tallent J.,
  Waterworth S.P. (2025)** — "Assessing the risk of low energy availability,
  bone mineral density and psychological strain in endurance athletes" —
  *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition*, 2025.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full text directly; title, full author list,
  journal, year confirmed, plus the participant ages and the quoted caveat.
  **Summary:** 55 endurance athletes — **23 females aged 45±13y**, 32 males
  aged 44±13y — with DXA lumbar-spine BMD, LEAF-Q, burnout and psychological
  strain measures. **77% of the female athletes flagged "at risk" on LEAF-Q**
  (vs 34-60% in prior, younger cohorts). Only **9% of females had low lumbar
  BMD** — and both of those women had a history of amenorrhea *and* a LEAF-Q
  score ≥8. 31% of males had low lumbar BMD.
  **The authors' own caveat, and the most important sentence in this
  dossier for Renee's demographic:** "The population in this study was older
  than in previous research, and some symptoms associated with LEA may be
  attributable to peri-menopausal changes in females. This could have
  artificially inflated LEAF-Q scores." They further note LEAF-Q was
  validated in elite athletes and "may be less sensitive in a more diverse
  population," and that "LEAF-Q thresholds have not been fully validated and
  may overestimate LEA risk."
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: medium (n=23 women,
  cross-sectional) — but it is **the closest study found to Renee's age
  band**, and its headline message is a warning about *false positives*, not
  a prevalence estimate to take at face value.

- **~ Sharp S., et al. (2025)** — "Assessment of relative energy deficiency in
  sport and malnutrition prevalence in female endurance runners by energy
  availability questionnaire, bioelectrical impedance analysis and
  relationship with ovulation status."
  **Verification:** the paper exists (publisher listing confirmed: 70 female
  runners from London clubs, median age 32, range 18-55) but the **journal
  title and full author list were not confirmed** — **~**, do not cite in a
  library file until confirmed.
  **Reported finding (secondary):** ~74% of younger (<35y) vs ~28% of
  masters (≥35y) runners flagged at increased RED-S risk by questionnaire.
  **Read with care:** the direction (lower flagged risk in masters) is the
  *opposite* of Gowers et al.'s older cohort (77% flagged). Two small studies
  in different cohorts with different instruments pointing opposite ways is
  the definition of "we don't know."

**What is genuinely not established for a peri-/post-menopausal athlete:**
1. **The canonical warning sign is gone.** Menstrual dysfunction is the
   primary indicator around which the Triad, LEAF-Q, and much of REDs
   screening were built. For a post-menopausal athlete it carries **zero**
   information. `Heikura et al. (2018)` concluded reproductive-hormone
   function was the *most reliable* marker available — that marker does not
   exist for her.
2. **No EA threshold has ever been derived in this population.** The 30
   kcal/kg number comes from young sedentary women (`Loucks & Thuma 2003`).
   Whether a different (higher? lower?) threshold applies to a
   post-menopausal athlete is **unstudied**.
3. **Symptom overlap is severe.** Fatigue, poor recovery, sleep disruption,
   mood change, GI complaints, low bone density and body-composition change
   are *all* features of both LEA **and** the menopause transition. There is
   no published way to disentangle them from questionnaire data — the Gowers
   authors say so about their own results.
4. **Bone risk stacks, and nobody has quantified the stack.** Post-menopausal
   bone loss (oestrogen withdrawal) + a non-osteogenic sport
   (`Gómez-Bruton et al. 2013/2018`) + any LEA-driven suppression of bone
   formation are three independent hits to the same tissue. **No study found
   examines this combination.** This is the largest unquantified risk in
   Renee's whole profile, and it is a **DXA + sports-physician question**,
   not a training-plan question.
5. **The two most-cited "consequences" papers exclude her age band
   entirely** (`Ackerman et al. 2019`: 15-30y; `Henninger et al. 2024`:
   18-40y).

### Topic 6 — Screening and detection: what a coach can and cannot infer

- **✓ Melin A., Tornberg Å.B., Skouby S., Faber J., Ritz C., Sjödin A.,
  Sundgot-Borgen J. (2014)** — "The LEAF questionnaire: a screening tool for
  the identification of female athletes at risk for the female athlete triad"
  — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 48(7):540-545.
  **Verification:** publisher/institutional listings; title, authors,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** The original LEAF-Q. Items cover injury history, GI symptoms,
  menstrual function and contraceptive use; a score ≥8 flags "at risk."
  Reported ~78% sensitivity / ~90% specificity **in its original validation
  cohort of elite female athletes**.
  **Tag:** screening-instrument evidence. Confidence: medium — **and note
  that its two strongest content domains (menstrual function, and to a
  degree injury) are exactly the ones that do not apply, or apply
  ambiguously, to a post-menopausal swimmer.**

- **✓ Rogers M.A., et al. (2021)** — "The Utility of the Low Energy
  Availability in Females Questionnaire to Detect Markers Consistent With Low
  Energy Availability-Related Conditions in a Mixed-Sport Cohort" —
  *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*,
  31(5).
  **Verification:** publisher and repository listings; title, lead author,
  journal, year, volume confirmed (full author list not enumerated —
  "et al." intentional; page range not confirmed).
  **Summary:** In a mixed-sport cohort, LEAF-Q subscales detected low BMD
  (100% sensitivity) and menstrual dysfunction (80% sensitivity) well, and
  negative predictive values were generally high (76.5-100%) — **but overall
  specificity was low**, so the total LEAF-Q score **cannot** be used as a
  diagnostic surrogate for LEA. The GI subscale detected nothing useful.
  Recommended use: **rule-out / low-risk triage**, not case-finding.
  **Tag:** screening-instrument evidence. Confidence: medium-high.
  **The rule this yields: a negative LEAF-Q is mildly reassuring; a positive
  LEAF-Q means "go get assessed", never "you have RED-S."**

- **✓ Klein D.J., McClain P., Montemorano V., Santacroce A. (2023)** —
  "Pre-Season Nutritional Intake and Prevalence of Low Energy Availability in
  NCAA Division III Collegiate Swimmers" — *Nutrients*, 15(13):2827.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full text directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/article number confirmed.
  **Summary:** 30 NCAA Division III swimmers (15 M, 15 F). **43% had
  measured EA below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day** (47% of men, 40% of women — no
  significant sex difference). Critically: **the LEAF-Q did not discriminate**
  female swimmers with vs without measured low EA — it correctly identified
  3 of the 6 low-EA women while false-flagging 3 who were not low-EA.
  Authors' own limitations: self-reported diet/activity (under-reporting),
  BIA over-estimates FFM in athletes (which *inflates* apparent LEA
  prevalence), small n.
  **Tag:** `[EVIDENCE: swim]` — the only swimmer-specific LEA study found.
  Confidence: medium (n=30, college-age, and the authors themselves flag
  that the prevalence figure may be inflated by BIA error).
  **Two things it establishes for this repo:** (1) LEA is *common* in
  swimmers, not a rarity — high volume + limited eating windows is a real
  exposure; (2) **the standard screening questionnaire failed in swimmers
  specifically.** The system should not lean on LEAF-Q-style scoring.

- **✓ Sterringer T., Larson-Meyer D.E. (2022)** — "RMR Ratio as a Surrogate
  Marker for Low Energy Availability" — *Current Nutrition Reports*,
  11:263-272.
  **Verification:** publisher listing; title, authors, journal, year,
  volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Reviews the ratio of *measured* RMR (indirect calorimetry) to
  *predicted* RMR as an LEA surrogate. Finds a reduced RMR ratio often
  co-occurs with LEA signs, **but** athletes with milder deficiency may show
  a normal ratio, and the measurement methodology is **not standardised**
  across studies. Recommends it only as a *complementary* measure.
  **Tag:** screening-instrument evidence. Confidence: medium.

- **✓ Espinar S., Martin-Olmedo J.J., Rueda-Córdoba M., Prado-Nóvoa O.,
  Contreras C., Martínez-Sanz J.M., Jurado-Fasoli L. (2025)** — "RMR and RMR
  ratio are not related to energy availability in elite and pre-elite
  athletes" — *Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism*, 50.
  **Verification:** publisher listing; title, full author list, journal, year
  confirmed.
  **Summary:** 49 competitive athletes (33 men), cross-sectional. **No
  significant association between EA and RMR or RMR-ratio.** (Athletes with
  RMR-ratio ≥0.9 did have better bone density, sleep regularity, lower
  stress and higher self-perceived performance — i.e. the ratio tracks
  *something* about overall condition, just not measured EA.)
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: medium (n=49,
  cross-sectional, mostly male).
  **Combined read with Sterringer & Larson-Meyer:** RMR ratio is **not** a
  validated LEA test. If a clinician orders it, fine. The app must not.

**What a coach / this system CAN legitimately do:**
- Notice **patterns over time in data it already has**: unexplained loss of
  durability at a given effort, RPE creep at matched pace, weight trending
  down during a build, recurrent illness, sleep and mood decline, a stalled
  or reversing long-swim progression.
- Ask about fuelling *adequacy* in an opening, non-restrictive way, and
  prompt for whether a very large training day was actually matched with
  food.
- **Escalate.** Say plainly: "this pattern is one a sports physician should
  look at."

**What it CANNOT do:** compute an EA number and act on it
(`Burke et al. 2018`); score a LEAF-Q and call it a finding
(`Rogers et al. 2021`; `Klein et al. 2023`); infer bone status from any
input it has (`Gómez-Bruton et al. 2013/2018` — only DXA answers this);
or interpret blood work. **Blood markers (T3, oestradiol, LH/FSH, ferritin,
lipids), DXA/BMD, and RMR by indirect calorimetry are all clinician-ordered
and clinician-interpreted.**

### Topic 7 — Is the swim/ultra/high-volume context a distinct risk profile?

- **✓ Grigg M.J., Thake C.D., Allgrove J.E., King J.A., Thackray A.E.,
  Stensel D.J., Owen A., Broom D.R. (2023)** — "Influence of water-based
  exercise on energy intake, appetite, and appetite-related hormones in
  adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis" — *Appetite*, 180:106375.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, full author list,
  journal, year, volume/article number confirmed.
  **Summary:** 8 acute studies. **No difference in ad-libitum energy intake
  between water-based and land-based exercise.** Water-based exercise
  *increased* intake vs a non-exercise control. **Cold water (18-20 °C)
  increased subsequent energy intake substantially more than neutral water
  (27-33 °C).** Appetite-hormone data were too inconsistent to pool.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (acute lab studies; several used
  submerged cycle ergometers, not swimming; mostly male, mostly young).
  Confidence: medium.
  **This kills a common assumption.** The idea that "swimming suppresses
  appetite and that's why swimmers under-fuel" is **not** supported. If
  anything the water — especially cold water — drives intake *up*. The
  swim-specific LEA hazard is structural, not appetite-mediated.

- **✓ White L.J., Dressendorfer R.H., Holland E., McCoy S.C., Ferguson M.A.
  (2005)** — "Increased caloric intake soon after exercise in cold water" —
  *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*,
  15(1):38-47.
  **Verification:** fetched PubMed record directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 11 men, randomised crossover, 45 min submerged cycle ergometer
  at 60% VO2max in 33 °C (neutral) vs 20 °C (cold) water vs rest. Post-
  exercise (1 h) energy intake was **~41-44% higher after the cold-water
  trial** than the neutral trial.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: low-medium as applied
  to Renee (n=11, all male, cycling not swimming, single acute bout, 20 °C is
  warmer than much of the open water she trains in).
  **Practical reading, and it is a kind one:** post-cold-swim ravenousness is
  a normal, documented physiological response. It is not a discipline
  failure and should not be resisted.

- **✓ Henninger K., Pritchett K., Brooke K., Dambacher L. (2024)** — "Low
  Energy Availability, Disordered Eating, Exercise Dependence, and Fueling
  Strategies in Trail Runners" — *International Journal of Exercise Science*,
  16(2):1471-1486.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full text directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 1,955 trail/ultra runners (1,445 women), **ages 18-40**.
  43% at LEA risk overall (49.7% of women); 43% at disordered-eating risk;
  87.3% showed exercise-dependence symptoms; 47.6% reported insufficient
  carbohydrate intake in events >2.5 h. LEAF-Q score correlated with
  disordered eating (r=0.29) and exercise dependence (r=0.33) — weak-to-
  moderate.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]` (ultra-endurance, but running; and the design
  is a self-report survey — the authors themselves list cross-sectional
  design, self-report bias, no validated male tool, and the 18-40 age
  restriction as limitations). Confidence: low-medium as evidence; useful as
  *prevalence context* only.
  **Read:** the ultra-endurance population *as a whole* shows a high flagged
  LEA rate. Combined with `Klein et al. (2023)` in swimmers (43% measured
  below threshold), the fair summary is: **high-volume endurance sport is a
  high-exposure context for under-fuelling.** That is a reason to take the
  question seriously — it is *not* a reason to assume Renee has RED-S.

**Synthesis of the swim-ultra risk profile (Coach judgment, labelled as
such):** the plausible swim-specific mechanisms are (1) very high exercise
energy expenditure per session; (2) sessions in which eating is mechanically
hard (you cannot casually snack mid-swim — see `08-ultra-feeding.md`);
(3) cold-water thermoregulation adding to the bill; (4) early-morning pool
practices creating long within-day deficit windows before the first real meal
(`Fahrenholtz et al. 2018`); and (5) **no osteogenic protection from the
sport itself** (`Gómez-Bruton et al. 2013/2018`) — so the bone consequence of
any deficit is unbuffered. **Appetite suppression is NOT one of the
mechanisms.** No study was found that tested this combination in
ultra-distance open-water swimmers; the above is inference.

### Topic 8 — What actually helps

- **✓ Cialdella-Kam L., Guebels C.P., Maddalozzo G.F., Manore M.M. (2014)** —
  "Dietary Intervention Restored Menses in Female Athletes with
  Exercise-Associated Menstrual Dysfunction with Limited Impact on Bone and
  Muscle Health" — *Nutrients*, 6(8).
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full text directly; title, authors, journal,
  year, volume confirmed.
  **Summary:** 8 female endurance athletes with exercise-associated menstrual
  dysfunction received **+360 kcal/day** (carbohydrate-protein supplement)
  for **6 months**; 10 eumenorrheic athletes served as controls. Menses
  resumed in a mean of 2.6 ± 2.2 months; **7 of 8 ovulated**. Women with
  <8 months of dysfunction recovered faster. **Bone mineral density barely
  moved, and muscle strength/power did not improve.**
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`. Confidence: **low-medium** as
  evidence (n=8, uncontrolled intervention) — but it is essentially the only
  prospective restoration study found, and its magnitude (+360 kcal/day) is
  a useful anchor for how *modest* the corrective dose can be.
  **Two honest takeaways:** (a) restoring energy availability need not mean a
  dramatic intervention — a few hundred kcal/day, consistently, was enough to
  restore reproductive function in most of these women; (b) **bone did not
  bounce back in 6 months.** Bone is the slow, partly-irreversible endpoint.
  That asymmetry is the whole argument for acting early rather than waiting
  for a stress fracture to settle the question.

- **✓ Kuikman M.A., Mountjoy M., Stellingwerff T., Burr J.F. (2021)** — "A
  Review of Nonpharmacological Strategies in the Treatment of Relative Energy
  Deficiency in Sport" — *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and
  Exercise Metabolism*, 31(3):268-275.
  **Verification:** publisher and repository listings; title, authors,
  journal, year, volume/pages confirmed. **Note:** a published *Commentary in
  Response* to this review exists (i.e. it was contested on publication) —
  another sign the treatment literature is not settled.
  **Summary:** Narrative review of pragmatic non-drug treatment levers:
  increase energy intake, address **low carbohydrate availability**
  specifically (not just total energy), fix **within-day** prolonged LEA
  windows, ensure bone-building nutrients, add **mechanical bone loading**,
  and reduce psychogenic stress.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium (narrative
  review; and treatment of a diagnosed case is a clinician's job, not this
  system's). **Included because it maps the levers, not to authorise the
  system to pull them.**

- **✓ Charlton B.T., Forsyth S., Clarke D.C. (2022)** — "Low Energy
  Availability and Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport: What Coaches Should
  Know" — *International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching*,
  17(2):445-460.
  **Verification:** publisher listing; title, authors, journal, year,
  volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Practitioner-facing review aimed at coaches. Notably reports
  that as few as **15% of coaches are aware of the Triad**, and up to 89%
  cannot name a single symptom.
  **Tag:** practitioner convention / educational review — **not** primary
  evidence. Confidence: n/a. Useful only to justify *why* a coaching system
  should surface this topic at all, and to set the coach's role as
  recognise-and-refer.

  number found and would be genuinely valuable if confirmed — but it points
  the *opposite* way from `Gowers et al. (2025)`, so it should not be used to
  reassure.
- **The "Protecting women's health in sport" (2026, Frontiers in Sports and
  Active Living) review** — fetched; despite promising search snippets, it
  does **not** address peri-/post-menopausal athletes. Don't chase it for the
  masters question.
- **ACC / Cycling Weekly / Physiopedia / me-pedia / clinic blog hits** — not
  peer-reviewed; not citable to `reference_list.md`.
- **Any RED-S prevalence figure quoted without its measurement method** —
  per `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)`, prevalence estimates in this field are
  extremely sensitive to measurement error and routinely inflated. Treat
  every "X% of athletes have LEA" headline as unreliable.
- **`Loucks & Thuma`'s 30 kcal/kg number as a clinical cut-off** — actively
  rejected here. Not an engine constant. Not a target to give the athlete.

## 4. Proposed claims for `library/13-reds-energy-availability.md`

Per `00-conventions.md`: ≤~2,500 words, every claim tagged, mark
**`UNREVIEWED`** until human-reviewed. Draft claims below in the library's
idiom. **Safety-relevant claims state the referral explicitly.**

**Claim 1 — The model, and what the engine must not do.**
> `Coach judgment:` Energy availability (intake minus exercise energy
> expenditure, per kg fat-free mass) is the organising concept behind
> RED-S/REDs. **The engine does not and will not compute an EA number.**
> `Burke, Lundy, Fahrenholtz & Melin (2018)` — themselves REDs consensus
> authors — document that field EA estimation has no standard protocol and
> carries substantial error in every term; `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)` show a
> reported 34% LEA prevalence collapsing to 5% after correcting for
> measurement error. Any EA figure this system produced would be noise
> dressed as a diagnosis.

**Claim 2 — The 30 kcal/kg FFM threshold is not settled and is not a target.**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: low-medium.** The threshold
> originates in `Loucks & Thuma (2003)` — 5 days, 29 **sedentary** young
> women, LH pulsatility. `Areta, Taylor & Koehler (2021)` state thresholds
> are "debated in females and unknown in males," and the **2023 IOC consensus
> itself abandoned fixed cut-offs** (`Mountjoy et al. 2023`) in favour of an
> adaptable-vs-problematic LEA spectrum. **Test:** none — this claim exists
> to *prevent* a test. If any future engine constant or coach reply quotes
> "30 kcal/kg" as a threshold Renee should hit, that is a bug.

**Claim 3 — Chronic LEA is a plausible upstream cause of durability loss, and
acute fuelling will not fix it.**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: medium-high.**
> `Caldwell et al. (2024)`: 12 endurance-trained **women**, randomised
> blinded crossover, 14 days at 22.3 vs 51.9 kcal/kg FFM/day — 20-min time
> trial impaired **7.8%**, and the impairment **persisted after 3 days of
> refuelling**; muscle glycogen and mitochondrial capacity were unchanged.
> **Test (concrete, against Renee's own data):** after in-session fuelling is
> corrected per `08-ultra-feeding.md`, track time-to-RPE-inflection on
> matched long sessions (.fit pace/HR drift + logged RPE) for 3-4 weeks. If
> durability at ~90 min does **not** improve despite corrected acute fuelling
> — and especially if it is accompanied by weight trending down, poor sleep,
> or repeat illness — **that pattern warrants a sports physician and a
> registered sports dietitian, not a training tweak.** The system should say
> exactly that, and should not offer a training adjustment as a substitute.

**Claim 4 — Swimming is not osteogenic; her bones get no protection from her
sport. This is the highest-value swim-specific finding here.**
> `[EVIDENCE: swim]` **Confidence: high.** `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2013)`
> (systematic review, 64 studies) and `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2018)`
> (meta-analysis) both find swimmers' BMD **similar to sedentary controls and
> lower than high-impact athletes**. Combined with post-menopausal bone loss
> and any LEA-driven suppression of bone formation, this is three
> independent hits on the same tissue — **a combination for which no study
> exists.**
> **This is a clinical question: a baseline DXA/bone-density assessment is a
> sports-physician conversation, and the system should recommend it rather
> than estimate around it.** `Hutson et al. (2021)` argue high-impact loading
> is osteogenic and energy-cheap and is under-used in endurance athletes —
> **Test:** adding brief impact/plyometric work to the existing 2×/week
> dryland block (`07-strength-dryland.md`) should cost near-zero extra
> session RPE or duration in her logs, and should not aggravate any joint
> complaint in her wellness entries. Whether it changes BMD is answerable
> **only** by repeat DXA, not by anything this app records.

**Claim 5 — The classic warning sign does not exist for this athlete.**
> `Coach judgment:` (grounded in `Heikura et al. 2018` and
> `Gowers et al. 2025`). Menstrual dysfunction is the primary indicator
> around which the Triad, LEAF-Q and much of REDs screening were built;
> `Heikura et al. (2018)` concluded reproductive-hormone function was the
> *most* reliable marker available and that dietary/training EA estimates
> disagreed with it. **For a peri-/post-menopausal athlete that marker
> carries no information**, and `Gowers et al. (2025)` — the only cohort
> found in her age band (females 45±13y) — explicitly warn that
> peri-menopausal symptoms may **artificially inflate** LEAF-Q scores.
> **Practical consequence: this system will not run a LEAF-Q-style score for
> her and will not treat its absence-of-flag as reassurance.** The honest
> statement is: *we have no validated screening instrument for your
> demographic.*

**Claim 6 — Screening instruments rule out; they do not rule in — and in
swimmers specifically, the standard one failed.**
> `[EVIDENCE: swim]` **Confidence: medium.** `Klein et al. (2023)`: 30 NCAA
> D-III swimmers; **43% measured below 30 kcal/kg FFM**, and the LEAF-Q
> **failed to discriminate** low-EA from adequate-EA women (3 of 6 caught,
> 3 false flags). Corroborated at the instrument level by
> `Rogers et al. (2021)`: high sensitivity/NPV, **low specificity** — useful
> to rule out, useless to diagnose. **Test:** none — this claim exists to
> stop the system from adopting a questionnaire score as a finding.

**Claim 7 — RMR ratio is not a validated LEA test.**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: medium.**
> `Sterringer & Larson-Meyer (2022)` call it promising but unstandardised and
> insensitive to milder deficiency; `Espinar et al. (2025)` found **no
> association** between EA and RMR or RMR-ratio in 49 competitive athletes.
> **Coach judgment:** if a clinician orders indirect calorimetry, good —
> but the app must not infer metabolic suppression from anything it has.

**Claim 8 — Within-day energy distribution is the one fuelling pattern this
system can legitimately notice.**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: medium.**
> `Fahrenholtz et al. (2018)`: in 25 elite female endurance athletes with
> **matched 24-hour EA**, those with menstrual dysfunction spent more of the
> day in deficit — timing, not just the daily total, tracked with
> dysfunction. **Test:** on Renee's biggest training days (top-decile
> .fit-derived load), check whether the first substantial intake follows the
> session by a long gap — e.g. an early pool practice with the first real
> meal hours later. A recurring long morning-deficit window is a *habit* the
> coach can raise **without computing an EA number and without implying a
> diagnosis**. Frame it as adding food earlier, never as an audit.

**Claim 9 — The swim/cold-water appetite folklore is backwards.**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: medium.**
> `Grigg et al. (2023)` (meta-analysis, 8 acute studies): water-based
> exercise does **not** suppress energy intake relative to land-based
> exercise and *increases* it vs rest; **cold water (18-20 °C) increases
> post-exercise intake substantially more than neutral water**
> (`White et al. 2005`, n=11 men, is the classic underlying trial).
> **Coach judgment:** post-cold-swim hunger is a documented physiological
> response, not a lapse. The system should never frame it as something to
> manage down. **Test:** none proposed — this claim exists to remove a
> harmful assumption, not to add a rule.

**Claim 10 — What restoring energy availability actually looks like (and its
limits).**
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: low-medium** (n=8,
> uncontrolled). `Cialdella-Kam et al. (2014)`: **+360 kcal/day for 6
> months** restored menses in 7 of 8 exercise-associated amenorrheic
> endurance athletes (mean 2.6 months to resumption), with **little change in
> bone or muscle outcomes over the same 6 months**. Two honest readings: the
> corrective dose can be modest and unglamorous; and **bone does not bounce
> back on the same timescale** — which is the argument for acting on a
> suspicion early rather than waiting for a fracture to settle it.
> **This is a description of what the literature shows, NOT a prescription.**
> Any actual intake target for Renee is a **registered sports dietitian's**
> call, informed by a **sports physician's** assessment.

**Claim 11 — The scope line, stated in the file itself.**
> `Coach judgment:` (grounded in `Stellingwerff et al. 2023`, whose REDs CAT2
> makes final diagnosis explicitly **physician-led**, and
> `Charlton et al. 2022` on the coach's recognise-and-refer role).
> **This system can flag a risk pattern and recommend evaluation. It cannot
> diagnose RED-S/REDs, cannot prescribe treatment, cannot interpret blood
> work or bone density, and must never talk anyone out of getting assessed.**
> Equally it must not fear-monger: LEA sits on a spectrum, the literature is
> actively contested (`Jeukendrup et al. 2024`;
> `De Souza, Williams, Nattiv et al. 2014`), and a flagged pattern is a
> reason to ask a doctor, not a verdict. If Renee asks "do I have RED-S?",
> the correct answer is: *I can't tell you that, and nothing in your logged
> data can — here is what I do see, and here is who can answer it.*

**Claim 12 — LEA would confound the recovery signals this system already
trusts.** (See §5 cross-references; this claim belongs in **both**
`13-*` and a cross-reference note in `10-recovery-hrv.md`.)
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` **Confidence: medium.**
> `Tegg et al. (2024)`'s meta-analysis found physically active women with
> secondary amenorrhea had **lower resting heart rate** and lower blood
> pressure alongside **impaired endothelial function and worse lipids** —
> i.e. an energy/hormonally-compromised state can present with wearable
> signals that a training system reads as *improving fitness*. **Coach
> judgment:** a falling resting HR is **not** self-evidently good news when
> it arrives alongside falling body mass, declining durability, or rising
> illness frequency. **Test:** if Renee's Oura RHR trends *down* while her
> `wellness_composite` and matched-effort pace/RPE trend the *wrong* way,
> the engine should treat that **divergence** as a flag rather than netting
> the two signals against each other — and should say so out loud.
> **Marker note:** `Tegg et al.` is currently **~** (author list unconfirmed)
> — verify before this claim is promoted out of `UNREVIEWED`.

## 5. Cross-reference notes

**→ `library/08-ultra-feeding.md` (acute in-session fuelling — companion
pass, in flight).** Strict division of labour: `08-*` owns grams-per-hour,
feed timing, carbohydrate type, gut training, the mechanics of feeding from a
kayak. `13-*` owns the *chronic* picture. The one place they must interlock
is the 90-minute-wall diagnostic: **`08-*` is the first hypothesis and the
cheaper fix; `13-*` is the fallback hypothesis if fixing acute fuelling
doesn't move the wall** (`Caldwell et al. 2024` — LEA-driven performance
decrement persisted through 3 days of refuelling, so it will not be rescued
by one well-fuelled session). Do not duplicate `08-*`'s numbers here.
`Kuikman et al. (2021)`'s point that **low carbohydrate availability** is a
distinct lever from total energy is the natural seam between the two files.

**→ `library/10-recovery-hrv.md` (already human-reviewed — read it).** Three
concrete interactions:
1. **RHR confound (Claim 12).** That file rates resting heart rate as the
   **highest-confidence** Oura signal (`Cao et al. 2022`; `Liang et al. 2024`;
   `Dial et al. 2025`) and says "treat an RHR trend as a real physiological
   signal first." That remains true about *measurement accuracy* — but
   `Tegg et al. (2024)` shows the *interpretation* is not
   monotonic: a lower RHR can accompany a genuinely harmful energy/hormonal
   state. `10-*` should carry a pointer: **RHR accuracy is high; RHR
   *meaning* is not always "fitter."**
2. **HRV under LEA is an open question I could not resolve.** I found no
   verified primary study of HRV in energy-deficient *athletes*. The
   physiology is genuinely ambiguous (cardiac vagal hyperactivity is
   documented in anorexia nervosa; whether it generalises to subclinical LEA
   in trained athletes is not established by anything I verified). **State
   this as a gap in both files rather than guessing a direction.** It matters
   because `10-*`'s forward-looking "hard day only if HRV stable-or-rising"
   rule assumes HRV moves the *right* way under stress — an assumption LEA
   may violate.
3. **`wellness_composite` is the right primary signal, and the REDs critics
   agree.** `Saw, Main & Gastin (2016)` already grounds the subjective-first
   design. `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)`'s proposed AHaRC alternative — assess
   eight independent stressor categories rather than attributing everything
   to energy — is structurally the same argument. The engine's existing
   multi-signal wellness design is closer to the *critics'* recommendation
   than to a single-cause energy model, and `13-*` should say so rather than
   proposing a new "LEA score."

**→ `library/07-strength-dryland.md`.** Claim 4's impact-loading proposal
(`Hutson et al. 2021`) lands in that file's territory. It is the one
*training-side* action in this whole dossier that is defensible without
resolving the RED-S debate at all — swimming is non-osteogenic regardless of
anyone's energy status, so impact loading is worth adding on its own merits.

**→ `library/03-periodization.md` / `adapt.py`.** No new engine constant is
proposed. The only engine-adjacent suggestion is a **divergence flag** (Claim
12): when objective trend and subjective/performance trend move in opposite
directions, surface it instead of averaging it away.

## 7. Gaps — stated bluntly

**The masters / peri-/post-menopausal female athlete is the biggest hole in
this literature, and Renee is standing in it.**

1. **No validated screening instrument exists for her.** LEAF-Q was built and
   validated around menstrual function in elite, menstruating athletes
   (`Melin et al. 2014`). For a post-menopausal athlete its central domain is
   uninformative; in the one masters-aged cohort found, the authors warn it
   likely produces **false positives** from peri-menopausal symptoms
   (`Gowers et al. 2025`). And in the one *swimmer* cohort found, it failed to
   discriminate at all (`Klein et al. 2023`). We have no instrument.
2. **No energy-availability threshold has ever been derived in her
   population.** The 30 kcal/kg figure is from 5 days in sedentary young women
   (`Loucks & Thuma 2003`). Nobody knows whether a post-menopausal, high-volume
   masters athlete's requirement sits higher, lower, or is even
   threshold-shaped.
3. **The canonical red flag is gone.** Amenorrhea — the signal `Heikura et al.
   (2018)` found *most reliable* — is not available. Nothing found in this pass
   proposes a validated substitute warning sign for post-menopausal athletes.
4. **The two most-cited consequence studies exclude her age band**
   (`Ackerman et al. 2019`: 15-30y; `Henninger et al. 2024`: 18-40y). The
   swim-specific performance study is in 15-17-year-olds
   (`VanHeest et al. 2014`). The bone meta-analysis is in 18-30-year-olds
   (`Gómez-Bruton et al. 2018`). **Essentially every headline finding in this
   dossier was generated in people at least two decades younger than her.**
5. **The bone-risk stack is unquantified.** Post-menopausal oestrogen
   withdrawal + a non-osteogenic sport + any LEA-driven bone-formation
   suppression is a three-way interaction **for which no study was found.**
   This is the one place where the honest answer is not "probably fine, keep
   an eye on it" but **"get a baseline DXA and talk to a sports physician."**
6. **HRV under LEA: unknown.** I could not verify a primary study of HRV in
   energy-deficient trained athletes. Given that the system is building toward
   HRV-guided load adjustment (`10-recovery-hrv.md`), this is a real blind
   spot — and `Tegg et al. (2024)`'s lower-RHR-with-worse-vascular-health
   finding suggests the direction cannot simply be assumed.
7. **No ultra-distance open-water swimming LEA evidence exists at all.** Not
   one study. Everything swim-specific here is pool-based
   (`Klein et al. 2023`; `VanHeest et al. 2014`;
   `Gómez-Bruton et al. 2013/2018`), and everything ultra-specific is
   running-based (`Henninger et al. 2024`). The combination is unstudied.
8. **The syndrome's own status is contested.** `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)` argue
   REDs as currently formulated may not meet the bar for a syndrome, and that
   the field's prevalence figures are inflated by measurement error. A library
   file that presents RED-S as settled would be misrepresenting the state of
   knowledge — and a coaching system that *diagnosed* from it would be doing
   something the IOC's own clinical tool says only a physician should do.

**Net honest statement for Renee, which the topic file should carry more or
less verbatim:** *Chronic low energy availability is a real and plausible
concern for someone training at your volume in a sport with no bone
protection. It is also a field with contested science, no validated screening
tool for your age group, and no evidence base in ultra-distance open-water
swimmers of any age. That combination means this system can tell you what
patterns it sees in your own data — and it means the actual answer has to come
from a sports physician and a registered sports dietitian, with a bone-density
scan as the concrete first step. This is not something a training app should
try to settle, in either direction.*
