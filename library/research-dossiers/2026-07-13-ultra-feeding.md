## 1. Executive summary

### The 90-minute wall

- **The athlete's framing contains a false premise, and this is the single
  most important finding in the pass.** ~90 minutes at moderate/steady
  intensity is **not** a physiologically plausible *muscle*-glycogen
  depletion point for a trained athlete eating normally. The classic
  time-to-exhaustion data put the muscle-glycogen wall at roughly **2–3
  hours**, not 90 minutes: Bergström et al. (1967) — exhaustion at 75%
  VO2max came at ~126 min on a normal mixed diet; Coggan & Coyle (1987) —
  trained cyclists fatigued at ~170 min at 70% VO2max. Confidence: high
  (`[ADAPTED: cycling]`, but this is foundational, replicated physiology).
- **And she is female, which pushes the muscle-glycogen wall *later*, not
  earlier.** Women oxidize proportionally more fat and less carbohydrate at
  matched relative intensity, with evidence of muscle-glycogen sparing
  (Tarnopolsky 2008). A sex-based prior therefore argues *against*
  substrate depletion at 90 min, not for it. Confidence: medium-high,
  `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`.
- **The glycogen hypothesis survives in exactly one form: low *starting*
  glycogen.** Bergström et al. (1967) is the key: identical athletes at the
  same 75% VO2max lasted **59 min** (low-carbohydrate diet), **126 min**
  (mixed diet), **189 min** (high-carbohydrate diet). A ~90 min wall sits
  squarely in the band you would predict for someone starting sessions with
  *below-normal* glycogen — i.e. a chronically under-carbed or
  fasted-training athlete. **This is the RED-S cross-link** (see §7).
- **A third mechanism neither the athlete nor the question considered, and
  it may be the best fit: liver glycogen / blood glucose, not muscle
  glycogen.** Liver glycogen is a much smaller store than muscle glycogen
  and is substantially depleted by an overnight fast (Gonzalez et al.
  2016). In Coggan & Coyle (1987), fatigue coincided with plasma glucose
  falling to 3.1 mmol/L, and was **reversed** by glucose infusion —
  fatigue was a blood-glucose problem. If she trains early-morning fasted
  (very common for coached pool squads), a 90-min "body is done" wall is
  far more consistent with hepatic/blood-glucose limitation than with
  muscle-glycogen depletion. Confidence: medium (mechanistically strong,
  but no swim data, and her fasted/fed status is unknown to this dossier —
  **check her logs first**).
- **The coincidence with her habitual session length is real evidence, and
  it points at the central/habituation account.** Substrate depletion has
  no way of knowing what time practice usually ends. Anticipatory-regulation
  work shows RPE is scaled to the *expected* endpoint, not just to
  afferent physiological feedback (Tucker 2009), and that manipulating
  expected duration changes RPE and pace with no change in the actual
  physiological demand (Baden et al. 2005; Eston et al. 2012). A wall that
  arrives at the same *clock time* as habitual practice — rather than at a
  fixed amount of *work done* — is the signature of a pacing/perceptual
  template, not an empty tank. Confidence: medium-high, `[ADAPTED:
  running/cycling]`.
- **Critical logical trap: "carbs fixed it" does NOT prove it was glycogen.**
  Two findings independently break that inference. (a) Coyle et al. (1986):
  carbohydrate feeding let cyclists ride an extra hour at 71% VO2max
  **without sparing muscle glycogen at all** — it works via blood glucose,
  not by refilling muscle. (b) Carbohydrate *mouth rinse* — swilling and
  spitting, zero calories absorbed — improves performance and lights up
  reward/motor brain regions on fMRI (Chambers et al. 2009; Carter et al.
  2004). So a feed can push back a wall through an oral/central route with
  no substrate contribution whatsoever. **Any test that just adds carbs and
  sees improvement is uninterpretable.** This is the crux of the
  experimental design in §3.

### Feeding

- **Duration-dose ladder** (per Jeukendrup 2014, already in
  `reference_list.md`): ~30 g/h for 1–2 h efforts; ~60 g/h for 2–3 h; up to
  **90 g/h for >2.5–3 h**, and only from multiple-transportable
  (glucose+fructose or maltodextrin+fructose) sources — single-transportable
  carbohydrate saturates the SGLT1 intestinal transporter at ~60 g/h. Note
  the implication for the 90-min question: **the literature's own dose
  guidance for a 90-minute session is ~30 g/h, i.e. it does not treat 90 min
  as a substrate-limited effort.** Confidence: high.
- **The gut is trainable and it is the real constraint.** Two weeks of
  repeated in-session carbohydrate exposure reduced GI symptoms and
  malabsorption and improved performance (Miall et al. 2018; framework in
  Jeukendrup 2017). 90 g/h is a *trained* capacity, not a starting one.
- **The swim-specific feeding constraint is genuinely under-researched, and
  the practice is practitioner convention, not evidence.** Marathon-swim
  convention (channel associations, Marathon Swimmers Forum, and the one
  Channel-swimmer survey found — see ⚠ below): feed every **20–30 min**
  (some at 45), **15–20 s per stop**, bottle-on-a-rope, vertical skulling.
  Time cost at a 30-min cadence ≈ 40 s/hour ≈ well under 1% of total time —
  **the time cost is real but small, and is not a good reason to feed less
  often.** No study was found that measured the performance cost of feed
  stops in swimming. Label this `Coach judgment:` / practitioner convention.
- **The feeding plan and the hyponatremia risk are the same problem.** To
  hit 90 g/h from an 8% drink you must take in ~1.1 L/h. Over a 9–12 h swim
  that is a large hypotonic fluid load, and overdrinking hypotonic fluid is
  *the* cause of exercise-associated hyponatremia. The resolution is to
  deliver carbohydrate in **more concentrated feeds** (higher g per mL) so
  the carbohydrate target does not drag a dangerous fluid volume behind it.
  This is coach judgment built on solid parts, not a studied protocol.

### Rehydration and EAH — **read §5 before advising anything**

- **Post-exercise rehydration, when a deficit is *measured*:** ingest
  ~**150% of the body-mass deficit** over the following ~4–6 h, in a drink
  containing **~40–60+ mmol/L sodium** — sodium is what makes the fluid
  stay in; plain water simply increases urine output (Shirreffs et al.
  1996; Merson et al. 2008; Evans et al. 2017). Typical sports drinks
  (~10–25 mmol/L) are **too dilute for post-exercise restoration**; oral
  rehydration solutions or "fluid + salty food" are the practical route.
  Spread it out — a large bolus drives a diuresis. Confidence: high,
  `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`.
- **The 150% rule is conditional and the condition is load-bearing.** It
  presumes a deficit you have actually *measured* by nude body mass.
  Applying "150%" to a deficit you assumed rather than weighed is precisely
  the overdrinking pathway into hyponatremia.
- **SAFETY — the athlete's reported symptom pattern is an
  exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) pattern until proven otherwise,
  and the intuitive response is the dangerous one.** Low urine output +
  persistent thirst after a long swim, *despite* electrolyte intake, cannot
  be read as "she needs more fluid." Three facts drive this:
  1. **Electrolyte intake does not rule EAH out.** The consensus position is
     explicit: sodium ingestion may attenuate the fall in blood sodium but
     **cannot prevent EAH in the setting of excessive fluid intake** — it is
     the amount of *fluid* ingested, not the amount of sodium, that sets the
     final blood sodium (Hew-Butler et al. 2017; Hew-Butler et al. 2015
     consensus). "But she was taking electrolytes" is not reassurance.
  2. **Low urine output is a listed EAH sign**, via non-osmotically driven
     arginine vasopressin (an SIADH-like picture) causing water retention —
     alongside weight gain, headache, nausea, confusion (Rosner 2019). It
     overlaps almost completely with the dehydration picture, and
     "provision of hypotonic fluids is appropriate for the dehydrated
     athlete, whereas such treatment could be detrimental for an athlete
     with EAH."
  3. **Her exact population is the highest-risk group in the only
     ultra-swim dataset that exists.** Wagner et al. (2012), 26.4 km
     open-water Marathon-Swim, Lake Zurich, 36 swimmers: EAH in **8% of
     males and 36% of females**; one symptomatic female at 127 mmol/L. And
     Rogers et al. (2015) is a case report of hyponatremic
     **encephalopathy** — seizures, serum sodium **119 mmol/L**, ICU,
     hypertonic saline — in a **woman after a 20 km open-ocean swim**. She
     recovered fully. This is not a theoretical risk for a 33.3 km female
     marathon swimmer; it is the documented failure mode.
- **Honest counterweight, because overstating this would also be a
  failure:** *persistent thirst* on its own leans mildly *against*
  hyponatremia — osmotic thirst should be **suppressed**, not heightened,
  when plasma is dilute. But it cannot exclude it: non-osmotic (volume-
  driven) thirst persists, and **hypovolemic EAH — hyponatremia with net
  weight loss — is described** (Hew-Butler et al. 2017). Symptoms are
  genuinely ambiguous and **cannot resolve this**.
- **Therefore, stated plainly: this symptom pattern is not a coaching-
  heuristic problem. It requires a serum sodium measurement — i.e. medical
  evaluation, not a hydration tweak.** The cheapest field discriminator is
  **nude body mass, post-void, before and after the swim**: mass *down* →
  a real deficit, rehydrate per protocol; mass *stable or up* + low urine
  output → **do not push fluids**, seek medical evaluation. If low urine
  output is ever accompanied by headache, nausea/vomiting, confusion,
  disorientation, or puffiness → urgent medical care, and withhold fluids
  pending it. Also worth asking: **NSAID use** is an EAH risk factor
  (Rosner 2019) and is common in masters athletes.
- **Sex as a risk factor is contested and the library must not overclaim
  it.** The swim data (Wagner 2012) show a 4.5× female excess, but the
  Boston Marathon analysis found the sex difference **disappeared** after
  adjusting for BMI and race time (discussed in Hew-Butler et al. 2017) —
  i.e. sex may be a proxy for body size and time-on-course. Honest position:
  being female is **at minimum not protective**, and in the only ultra-swim
  cohort it tracked with substantially higher risk.
- **Immersion itself changes fluid regulation, and this is why swim
  hydration cannot be reasoned about like running hydration.** Head-out
  immersion shifts ~700 mL of blood centrally via hydrostatic pressure,
  suppressing vasopressin and driving a diuresis and natriuresis (Epstein
  1992). Two consequences: (a) she urinates *during* the swim, so post-swim
  body-mass loss **overestimates sweat loss** — a methodological problem
  that also undercuts published swim "sweat rate" figures; (b) on exit, the
  hydrostatic squeeze is removed, central volume falls, and vasopressin
  rebounds — a plausible mechanism for post-swim water retention and low
  urine output. **(b) is mechanistic inference by this dossier, not a
  demonstrated finding — mark it low confidence and do not present it as
  established.**
- **Warm water (Greece) genuinely does raise fluid need — do not
  over-rotate into fluid restriction.** Warm-water open-water racing
  produces significant thermal strain (Chalmers et al. 2021), and one
  recent (low-impact-journal, ~) study reports sweat rates near 2 L/h in
  warm-water 10 km racing. Meanwhile pool swimmers' sweat rates are low
  (~0.1–0.3 L/h; Cox et al. 2002). The correct posture is **measure, don't
  guess**: her Greece fluid needs are not her pool fluid needs and are not
  a cold-water Channel swimmer's needs.

## 2. Verified sources by topic

### Topic 1 — Glycogen depletion timeline: when does it actually bite?

- **✓ Bergström J., Hermansen L., Hultman E., Saltin B. (1967)** — "Diet,
  Muscle Glycogen and Physical Performance" — *Acta Physiologica
  Scandinavica*, 71:140–150.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed across the publisher listing and independent citation records;
  headline numbers confirmed.
  **Summary:** 9 subjects, needle-biopsy muscle glycogen, cycling to
  exhaustion at 75% VO2max after three diets. Starting muscle glycogen was
  manipulated from ~0.6 to ~4.7 g/100 g muscle. Time to exhaustion averaged
  **59 min** (fat/protein, low-carb), **126 min** (mixed), **189 min**
  (carbohydrate-rich), with a good correlation between work time and
  starting glycogen. Small n, 1967, male, cycling.
  **Why it matters here:** this is the paper that both kills and rescues the
  glycogen hypothesis. On a normal diet the wall is ~2 h, not 90 min — but
  with depleted starting glycogen it moves to ~1 h. The athlete's 90-min
  wall is only a glycogen story **if her starting glycogen is low**.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: high (foundational, replicated).

- **✓ Coggan A.R., Coyle E.F. (1987)** — "Reversal of fatigue during
  prolonged exercise by carbohydrate infusion or ingestion" — *Journal of
  Applied Physiology*, 63(6):2388–2395.
  **Verification:** title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed
  via publisher and PubMed listings; numbers confirmed.
  **Summary:** 7 cyclists rode at 70% VO2max to fatigue (~170 min), during
  which plasma glucose fell from 5.0 to **3.1 mmol/L** and RER fell from
  0.87 to 0.81. After a 20-min rest they resumed on placebo, ingested
  glucose polymer, or IV glucose infusion. Time to fatigue in the second
  bout: 10 min (placebo), 26 min (ingestion), **43 min (infusion)**.
  Fatigue was attributed primarily to inadequate carbohydrate supply to
  working muscle and was reversible by restoring blood glucose.
  **Why it matters:** (a) sets the fatigue point at ~2.8 h, not 1.5 h;
  (b) demonstrates the fatigue was a **blood-glucose** phenomenon — the
  strongest support for the liver-glycogen/hypoglycemia mechanism as the
  overlooked third explanation.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: high. Small n, male.

- **✓ Coyle E.F., Coggan A.R., Hemmert M.K., Ivy J.L. (1986)** — "Muscle
  glycogen utilization during prolonged strenuous exercise when fed
  carbohydrate" — *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 61(1):165–172.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed via publisher and PubMed listings.
  **Summary:** 7 endurance-trained cyclists at 71% VO2max to fatigue, with
  placebo vs. glucose-polymer feeding (2.0 g/kg at 20 min, then 0.4 g/kg
  every 20 min). Carbohydrate feeding delayed fatigue by roughly an hour —
  and **did not spare muscle glycogen**. Fed cyclists completed the extra
  hour while relying on blood glucose rather than muscle glycogen.
  **Why it matters:** this is the paper that makes "the feed fixed it,
  therefore it was glycogen" an invalid inference. Feeding works
  *downstream* of muscle glycogen.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: high.

- **✓ Gonzalez J.T., Fuchs C.J., Betts J.A., van Loon L.J.C. (2016)** —
  "Liver glycogen metabolism during and after prolonged endurance-type
  exercise" — *American Journal of Physiology — Endocrinology and
  Metabolism*, 311(3):E543–E553.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed via publisher and institutional repository listings.
  **Summary:** Review. Endogenous carbohydrate stores (liver + muscle) are
  small relative to the demands of prolonged exercise, and endurance
  capacity is often limited by endogenous carbohydrate availability. Notes
  that exercise-metabolism research has focused overwhelmingly on *muscle*
  glycogen with comparatively little attention to *liver* glycogen, despite
  liver glycogen's role in maintaining euglycemia; 13-C MRS now allows
  non-invasive measurement.
  **Why it matters:** grounds the "it may be liver, not muscle" hypothesis,
  and specifically the fasted-morning-training scenario.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium-high (review;
  the specific fasted-swimmer application is inference).
  **Caveat for the build agent:** I did not obtain the review's exact
  gram-figures for liver glycogen content from a primary read (the
  publisher blocked full-text fetch). **Do not put a specific liver-glycogen
  gram number in the library file on the strength of this dossier** — cite
  it qualitatively ("a much smaller store than muscle glycogen, and
  substantially depleted by an overnight fast") or verify the number first.

- **✓ Areta J.L., Hopkins W.G. (2018)** — "Skeletal Muscle Glycogen Content
  at Rest and During Endurance Exercise in Humans: A Meta-Analysis" —
  *Sports Medicine*, 48(9):2091–2102.
  **Verification:** title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed
  via publisher and PubMed listings.
  **Summary:** Meta-analysis establishing normative muscle-glycogen
  concentrations at rest and during exercise, and how they are modified by
  training status, acute carbohydrate availability, and exercise
  duration/intensity — motivated explicitly by the absence of normative
  values in the literature.
  **Why it matters:** the reference source for "what does a normal athlete's
  glycogen actually do over 90 min", and for the claim that starting
  glycogen is diet-dependent.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: high (meta-analysis).
  **Caveat:** I verified the paper's existence, scope and framing but did
  **not** extract specific numeric thresholds from a primary read. Cite
  qualitatively unless verified.

- **✓ Tarnopolsky M.A. (2008)** — "Sex Differences in Exercise Metabolism and
  the Role of 17-Beta Estradiol" — *Medicine & Science in Sports &
  Exercise*, 40(4):648–654.
  **Verification:** title, author, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed via
  publisher listing.
  **Summary:** Review. Women oxidize proportionally more lipid and less
  carbohydrate (and less protein) than men during endurance exercise, with
  higher intramyocellular lipid content/use and greater adipocyte lipolysis;
  glucose rate of appearance/disappearance is lower in women, with evidence
  of muscle-glycogen sparing and lower hepatic glucose production.
  Estradiol appears causal (E2 administration spares muscle and liver
  glycogen).
  **Why it matters:** the sex-based prior runs *against* the athlete's
  glycogen hypothesis. Also a caution: nearly every glycogen-timeline study
  cited above is male.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium-high (review;
  effect sizes vary by population, and are less pronounced in trained
  athletes).

### Topic 2 — The competing explanation: a habituated/perceptual duration ceiling

- **✓ Tucker R. (2009)** — "The anticipatory regulation of performance: the
  physiological basis for pacing strategies and the development of a
  perception-based model for exercise performance" — *British Journal of
  Sports Medicine*, 43(6):392–400.
  **Verification:** title, author, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed via
  PubMed and multiple independent citation records.
  **Summary:** Theoretical/review paper. Work rate in self-paced exercise is
  regulated by the brain integrating feedforward (expected endpoint) and
  feedback (afferent) signals. RPE is generated from afferent signals and
  then **matched against a subconscious RPE template** scaled to the
  expected duration; pace is adjusted to keep the athlete on that template.
  **Why it matters:** provides the mechanism for a duration ceiling that
  tracks *habit* rather than substrate. A model, not an experiment —
  and the "central governor" family of models is **contested** in the
  literature (Marcora's psychobiological model is the main rival). Say so.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running/cycling]`, Confidence: medium (influential but
  actively debated theory).

- **✓ Baden D.A., McLean T.L., Tucker R., Noakes T.D., St Clair Gibson A.
  (2005)** — "Effect of anticipation during unknown or unexpected exercise
  duration on rating of perceived exertion, affect, and physiological
  function" — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 39(10):742–746.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed via PubMed listing.
  **Summary:** Runners ran at fixed treadmill speed under different duration
  expectations (known 20 min, unknown, and a 10-min trial unexpectedly
  extended). RPE and affect changed sharply at the moment the *expectation*
  changed, **with treadmill speed and physiological demand held constant**.
  Being told the run would continue past the expected endpoint spiked RPE
  and worsened affect with no physiological change.
  **Why it matters:** this is the cleanest demonstration that a "wall" can be
  generated by expectation alone. It is the direct experimental analogue of
  "her body is done at exactly the time practice usually ends."
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]`, Confidence: medium-high (small, but the
  design isolates expectation from physiology — exactly the confound at
  issue).

- **~ Eston R. et al. (2012)** — "Effect of deception and expected exercise
  duration on psychological and physiological variables during treadmill
  running and cycling" — *Psychophysiology*, 49(4).
  **Verification:** title, journal, year and lead author confirmed via
  publisher and PubMed listings; **full author list and exact pages not
  individually confirmed** — hence `~`.
  **Summary:** Deception about task duration altered RPE and pacing without
  corresponding physiological justification; revealing the deception raised
  RPE and slowed pace, interpreted as disruption of the
  feedforward/feedback regulation loop.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running/cycling]`, Confidence: medium. Corroborates
  Baden (2005) from an independent group — the pair is the reason this
  explanation deserves equal billing with glycogen, not a footnote.

- **✓ Chambers E.S., Bridge M.W., Jones D.A. (2009)** — "Carbohydrate
  sensing in the human mouth: effects on exercise performance and brain
  activity" — *The Journal of Physiology*, 587(8):1779–1794.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed via publisher, institutional repository, and citation records.
  **Summary:** Rinsing (not swallowing) glucose or maltodextrin solutions —
  disguised with artificial sweetener so sweetness was not the cue —
  improved cycling time-trial performance vs. placebo, and fMRI localised
  increased activation in primary taste cortex and reward/limbic regions.
  Effect is independent of sweetness and of any absorbed calories.
  **Why it matters:** proves an oral/central route by which carbohydrate
  improves performance with **zero** substrate delivery. This is what makes
  a naive feeding experiment uninterpretable — and what makes a mouth-rinse
  arm the right control (§3).
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: high for the mechanism.
  **Caveat:** mouth-rinse benefits are most reliably shown in efforts
  **under ~1 h**, often in fasted subjects; the effect is smaller/less
  consistent in fed athletes and longer events. Do not oversell it as an
  ergogenic aid for a 33 km swim — its value here is **diagnostic**.

- **~ Carter J.M., Jeukendrup A.E., Jones D.A. (2004)** — "The effect of
  carbohydrate mouth rinse on 1-h cycle time trial performance" — *Medicine
  & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 36(12).
  **Verification:** title, authors, journal and year corroborated across
  secondary reviews and citation listings (including Jeukendrup's own review
  work); **not individually fetched from the primary source** — hence `~`.
  **Summary:** The original mouth-rinse study: a carbohydrate mouth rinse
  (not ingested) produced performance benefits comparable to ingestion in a
  1-h cycling time trial, which is the finding that prompted the "central
  effect" hypothesis later confirmed by Chambers et al. (2009).
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: medium (verify before making it
  load-bearing; Chambers 2009 is the stronger ✓ citation for the same point
  and should lead).

### Topic 3 — In-exercise carbohydrate feeding: rates, transporters, gut training

*(Note: `reference_list.md` already carries Jeukendrup (2014) — the
personalized-carbohydrate-intake review with the ~90 g/h multi-transportable
guidance and the "prescribe in absolute grams, not per kg" point — plus
Shaw et al. (2014) and Martinez-Sanz et al. (2024). Those are the spine of
this section and do **not** need re-verification. The entries below are the
new material.)*

- **✓ Jeukendrup A.E. (2017)** — "Training the Gut for Athletes" — *Sports
  Medicine*, 47(Suppl 1):101–110.
  **Verification:** title, author, journal, year, supplement/pages confirmed
  via publisher and PMC listings.
  **Summary:** Review. The GI tract is a major performance determinant in
  prolonged exercise and GI symptoms are common. Critically, **the gut is
  adaptable**: gastric emptying, stomach comfort, and intestinal nutrient
  absorption can all be trained, and the adaptations are **nutrient-
  specific** (repeated carbohydrate exposure upregulates the relevant
  intestinal transporters). Practical recommendation: regularly (at least
  weekly) rehearse the competition feeding strategy during training.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium-high (review by
  the field's leading author; the mechanistic transporter claims rest on a
  modest primary literature).
  **COI note:** Jeukendrup has extensive, long-standing commercial ties to
  the sports-nutrition industry (including sports-drink manufacturers).
  The 90 g/h recommendation is not thereby wrong — it is well replicated —
  but the field's central recommendations largely originate from
  industry-adjacent labs, and the library should say so.

- **✓ Miall A., Khoo A., Rauch C., Snipe R.M.J., Camões-Costa V.L.,
  Gibson P.R., Costa R.J.S. (2018)** — "Two weeks of repetitive gut-challenge
  reduce exercise-associated gastrointestinal symptoms and malabsorption" —
  *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, 28(2):630–640.
  **Verification:** title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages
  confirmed via the authors' institutional research portal and citation
  records.
  **Summary:** Two weeks of repeated in-run carbohydrate challenge (vs.
  placebo) improved GI symptoms and running performance; the carbohydrate
  supplement condition also **reduced malabsorption and increased blood
  glucose availability** during endurance running. Small, running-based.
  **Why it matters:** the actual experimental evidence that gut training
  works, as opposed to Jeukendrup (2017)'s framing of it. **90 g/h is a
  trained capacity — she should build to it, not attempt it on race day.**
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]`, Confidence: medium (single small study, but
  it directly tests the intervention).

## 3. The discriminating test (the actual deliverable for question 1)

The two hypotheses make **different predictions**, and they can be separated
with her existing `.fit`-derived pace/HR/pause analytics plus three cheap
session manipulations. The design matters because, as established above, a
naive "feed her carbs and see" experiment **cannot** distinguish them —
carbohydrate improves performance through an oral/central route with no
substrate delivery at all (Chambers 2009).

**Prediction table:**

| Observation | Substrate (glycogen/glucose) | Habituated ceiling |
|---|---|---|
| Wall timing across sessions of **different intensity** | tracks **work done**, so arrives *later* in an easy session | tracks the **clock**, so arrives at ~90 min regardless |
| Wall timing when she is **told in advance** the session is 2.5 h | unchanged | **moves or disappears** |
| Response to a **non-caloric sweet mouth-rinse** | no benefit | benefit (central route intact) |
| Response to **real carbohydrate** | benefit | benefit (uninterpretable alone!) |
| **HR at the wall** (from `.fit`) | HR **falls** or fails to rise with effort; pace decays; RPE soars | pace decays with **HR maintained or drifting up** — an effort/output decoupling |
| Sensitivity to **pre-session carbohydrate / fasted vs fed** | large effect | little effect |

**The four sessions to run (in this order):**
1. **Fed vs. fasted, matched steady session.** If the wall is
   liver-glycogen/blood-glucose, this alone may resolve it — and it is the
   cheapest test in the set. (Grounded in Coggan & Coyle 1987; Gonzalez et
   al. 2016.)
2. **Intensity manipulation.** A deliberately *easy* long swim. If the wall
   still lands at ~90 min despite far less work done, substrate depletion is
   effectively excluded. (Grounded in Bergström 1967 — the wall is a
   function of glycogen used, not minutes elapsed.)
3. **Expectation manipulation.** Tell her in advance the session is 2.5 h,
   with no feeding change. If the 90-min wall softens or moves, that is the
   Baden (2005) effect in her own data.
4. **Mouth-rinse control.** Real carbohydrate feed vs. non-caloric
   sweet-matched mouth rinse vs. nothing. If rinse ≈ feed, the benefit is
   central, not substrate. (Grounded in Chambers 2009; Carter 2004.)

**Honest caveats on this design:** she cannot be blinded to fasted/fed or to
duration expectation, so 1 and 3 carry a strong placebo/expectancy channel —
which is precisely the variable under test in (3) and a confound in (1).
n=1, unblinded, order effects, and day-to-day wellness variance will be
large relative to the effects. This is a **structured coaching probe, not an
experiment**; the library file should say so rather than dressing it up.

**And the most likely answer, stated as coach judgment:** these are not
mutually exclusive. The clean-looking coincidence of the wall with her
habitual session length points at habituation; a genuinely low starting
glycogen (from chronic under-fuelling and/or fasted morning training) would
lower the physiological ceiling *and* teach the perceptual template *at the
same time*, at the same 90-minute mark. **Under-fuelling is the mechanism
that would generate both explanations simultaneously**, which is why the
RED-S file (§7) and this one have to be read together.

### On the 90-minute wall

> **A ~90-minute wall is not, by itself, evidence of muscle-glycogen
> depletion.** Time-to-exhaustion at 70–75% VO2max is ~2 hours on a normal
> mixed diet and ~2.8 hours in trained athletes (per Bergström et al. (1967)
> and Coggan & Coyle (1987)); female athletes oxidize proportionally more
> fat and show muscle-glycogen sparing relative to males (per Tarnopolsky
> (2008)), which moves the expected wall later still.
> `[ADAPTED: cycling]`
> Confidence: high
> Test: compare the athlete's wall timing across sessions of clearly
> different intensity. True substrate depletion tracks **work done**, so it
> must arrive *later* in a low-intensity session. If the "done" point lands
> at ~90 min in both a hard session and an easy one, glycogen depletion is
> excluded — check `.fit` elapsed-time-at-onset against total work/TSS for
> each session.

> **The glycogen explanation survives only if her *starting* glycogen is
> low.** In the same athletes at the same intensity, time to exhaustion fell
> to ~59 min on a low-carbohydrate diet vs. ~126 min on a mixed diet (per
> Bergström et al. (1967)). A ~90-minute wall is what you would predict for
> an athlete beginning sessions under-carbed.
> `[ADAPTED: cycling]`
> Confidence: medium-high
> Test: log carbohydrate intake in the 24 h before each session and whether
> the session was fasted. If the wall's timing correlates with pre-session
> carbohydrate availability, this is a fuelling problem, not a ceiling.
> **Cross-reference `09-red-s.md` — chronic low energy/carbohydrate
> availability is the mechanism that would produce this.**

> **A ~90-minute wall in a fasted morning session is more consistent with
> liver-glycogen/blood-glucose limitation than with muscle-glycogen
> depletion.** Liver glycogen is a much smaller store than muscle glycogen
> and is substantially depleted by an overnight fast (per Gonzalez et al.
> (2016)); fatigue in prolonged exercise coincides with falling plasma
> glucose and is **reversed** by restoring blood glucose (per Coggan &
> Coyle (1987)).
> `[ADAPTED: cycling]`
> Confidence: medium
> Test: run one matched session fed (~1 g/kg carbohydrate, 1–2 h prior) and
> one fasted. If the wall moves substantially, this is a blood-glucose
> problem with a trivially cheap fix — and it should be ruled out **before**
> any elaborate duration-ceiling intervention.

> **A wall that arrives at the athlete's habitual session length, regardless
> of the work done, is the signature of a perceptual/pacing template, not an
> empty tank.** RPE is scaled against a subconscious template keyed to the
> *expected* endpoint (per Tucker (2009)); manipulating expected duration
> changes RPE, affect and pace with the physiological demand held constant
> (per Baden et al. (2005) and Eston et al. (2012)).
> `[ADAPTED: running]`
> Confidence: medium-high (note: the underlying anticipatory-regulation /
> "central governor" model is **contested** in the literature — the finding
> that expectation moves RPE is solid; the model built on top of it is not
> settled)
> Test: tell her in advance that a session is 2.5 h rather than her usual
> ~90 min, changing nothing else. If the wall softens, moves, or
> disappears, the ceiling is at least partly perceptual. Then progress
> session duration deliberately (a "long swim ladder" already exists in
> `06-long-swim-progression.md` — the same +≤15% cap applies).

> **"Carbohydrate made the wall go away" does NOT prove the wall was
> substrate depletion.** Carbohydrate feeding delays fatigue **without
> sparing muscle glycogen** (per Coyle et al. (1986)), and a carbohydrate
> *mouth rinse* — swilled and spat, zero calories absorbed — improves
> performance and activates reward and motor regions on fMRI (per Chambers
> et al. (2009)).
> `[ADAPTED: cycling]`
> Confidence: high
> Test: the only interpretable feeding trial includes a **non-caloric
> sweet-matched mouth-rinse arm**. If rinse ≈ real feed, the benefit is
> central/oral, not substrate — and the intervention is a duration
> progression, not a nutrition plan.

### On in-session feeding

> **For efforts over ~2.5–3 h, target up to 90 g/h of carbohydrate from
> multiple-transportable sources (glucose+fructose or maltodextrin+fructose);
> single-transportable carbohydrate saturates intestinal transport at ~60
> g/h.** Below that duration the dose ladder is ~30 g/h (1–2 h) and ~60 g/h
> (2–3 h) — note that **the literature's own prescription for a 90-minute
> session is ~30 g/h, i.e. it does not treat 90 minutes as a substrate-
> limited effort.** (Per Jeukendrup (2014) and Shaw et al. (2014), both
> already in `reference_list.md`.)
> `[EVIDENCE: swim]` for the open-water application (Shaw is a swim review);
> `[ADAPTED: cycling]` for the transporter physiology
> Confidence: high
> Test: for the 33.3 km target (~9–12 h), the operative band is 60–90 g/h.
> Verify tolerance on long open-water swims, logging GI symptoms against
> g/h actually consumed — not planned.

> **90 g/h is a trained capacity, not a starting one — the gut adapts, and
> it must be trained.** Two weeks of repeated in-session carbohydrate
> exposure reduced GI symptoms **and** carbohydrate malabsorption and
> improved performance (per Miall et al. (2018)); the gut's absorptive and
> gastric-emptying capacity is nutrient-specifically trainable (per
> Jeukendrup (2017)).
> `[ADAPTED: running]`
> Confidence: medium-high
> Test: rehearse the exact race feed (same product, same concentration, same
> cadence) on **every** long open-water swim, and track GI symptom scores
> across the block. Expect tolerance to improve over ~2+ weeks of repeated
> exposure. If GI symptoms are *not* improving with exposure, the feed
> composition or concentration — not her gut — is the problem.
> **COI note for the file: the carbohydrate-dose literature originates
> substantially from industry-funded and industry-adjacent labs. The
> findings are well replicated; the funding context should be stated.**

> **Feeding a swimmer costs time, but far less than swimmers assume — the
> real constraint is the gut and the fluid volume, not the clock.**
> Marathon-swim convention is a feed every 20–30 minutes taking 15–20
> seconds (bottle on a rope, vertical skulling) — roughly 40 s/hour, well
> under 1% of total swim time.
> `Coach judgment:` — **practitioner convention (channel-swimming
> associations and marathon-swim community practice); no research was found
> that measured the performance cost of feed stops in swimming, and the
> only survey of Channel swimmers' feeding behaviour could not be verified
> as a peer-reviewed source.**

> **Do not let the carbohydrate target drag a dangerous fluid volume behind
> it.** Delivering 90 g/h from a dilute (~8%) drink requires ~1.1 L/h; over
> a 9–12 h swim that is exactly the hypotonic fluid load that causes
> exercise-associated hyponatremia (see the safety section). Hit the
> carbohydrate target with **more concentrated feeds**, not more fluid.
> `Coach judgment:` (assembled from Jeukendrup (2014) on dose and
> Hew-Butler et al. (2017) on fluid volume; **this specific combination has
> not been studied**)
> Confidence: low-medium — flag explicitly as reasoning, not evidence.
> Test: compute litres/hour implied by her planned feed schedule *before*
> the swim; compare against her measured body-mass change on long training
> swims (weigh nude, post-void, before and after). If planned intake exceeds
> measured losses, the plan is wrong.

### On post-swim rehydration

> **When a fluid deficit has been *measured*, replace ~150% of the body-mass
> loss over the following ~4–6 hours, using a drink or meal delivering
> ~40–60+ mmol/L sodium.** Volume alone is insufficient — without adequate
> sodium the excess is simply urinated out (per Shirreffs et al. (1996),
> Merson et al. (2008), Evans et al. (2017)). Most commercial sports drinks
> (~10–25 mmol/L sodium) are **too dilute for post-exercise restoration**;
> an oral rehydration solution, or fluid taken with salty food, is the
> practical route. Spread intake over hours — a large bolus provokes a
> diuresis.
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`
> Confidence: high (for the protocol) — but note every underlying study is
> small-n, male, cycling-based, and measures a ~2% deficit, not a 4-hour
> open-water swim.
> Test: weigh nude and post-void immediately before and after long swims.
> Multiply the deficit by 1.5 for the replacement volume. If there is no
> deficit, **there is nothing to replace** — see the safety rail below.

> **The 150% rule is conditional on having measured the deficit. Applying it
> to an assumed deficit is the overdrinking pathway into hyponatremia.**
> Both hypohydration and **hyperhydration** compromise performance and
> health (per McDermott et al. (2017)).
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`
> Confidence: high

### SAFETY RAIL — exercise-associated hyponatremia

> **Low urine output and persistent thirst after a long swim — *despite*
> electrolyte intake — is an ambiguous symptom pattern that includes
> exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH), and the intuitive response
> ("drink more") is the one that can kill. This is a medical question, not
> a coaching one. It requires a serum sodium measurement.**
>
> - **Electrolyte intake does not exclude EAH.** Sodium ingestion may
>   attenuate the fall in blood sodium but **cannot prevent EAH in the
>   setting of excessive fluid intake** — it is the volume of *fluid*, not
>   the *sodium*, that drives the final blood sodium (per Hew-Butler et al.
>   (2017); Hew-Butler et al. (2015) consensus statement).
> - **Low urine output is a listed EAH sign**, via inappropriate
>   (non-osmotic) vasopressin secretion causing water retention, alongside
>   headache, nausea, vomiting, confusion and weight gain — and it
>   **overlaps almost completely with the dehydration picture**. Giving
>   hypotonic fluid is correct for the dehydrated athlete and **detrimental**
>   for the athlete with EAH (per Rosner (2019)).
> - **This athlete's population carries the highest reported ultra-swim
>   risk.** In a 26.4 km open-water swim, EAH occurred in **36% of female
>   and 8% of male** swimmers (per Wagner et al. (2012)); a woman completing
>   a 20 km open-ocean swim presented with **seizures and a serum sodium of
>   119 mmol/L**, requiring ICU care and hypertonic saline (per Rogers et al.
>   (2015)).
> `[EVIDENCE: swim-ultra]` (Wagner 2012 and Rogers 2015 are both female
> ultra-distance open-water swimmers — this is a genuine swim-ultra-tier
> safety claim, not an adaptation)
> Confidence: high that the risk is real and population-matched; the
> *incidence* estimate rests on a single 36-person event.
>
> **What the coach does — and does not — do:**
> 1. **Do not tell her to drink more.** Not as a default, not as a
>    "probably fine" hedge.
> 2. **Weigh nude, post-void, before and after every long swim.** Mass
>    **down** → a real deficit; rehydrate per the 150% protocol. Mass
>    **stable or up** with low urine output → **withhold extra fluid and
>    refer for medical evaluation.**
> 3. **Escalate immediately** — urgent medical care, no fluids — if low
>    urine output is accompanied by headache, nausea/vomiting, confusion,
>    disorientation, unusual fatigue, or puffiness/swelling.
> 4. **Ask about NSAIDs.** NSAID use is an EAH risk factor and is common in
>    masters athletes (per Rosner (2019)).
> 5. **Recurrence is the trigger for a serum sodium test.** Symptoms cannot
>    settle this; only a blood test can.
> Test: **none.** This is not a claim to falsify against her training data.
> It is a referral rule. `00-conventions.md`'s **acute-symptom override**
> applies — the tagging scheme does not govern here.

> **Honest limits of the above, so the file doesn't overclaim:**
> - *Persistent thirst* on its own leans mildly **against** hyponatremia
>   (osmotic thirst should be *suppressed* when plasma is dilute) — but it
>   cannot exclude it, because volume-driven thirst persists and
>   **hypovolemic EAH (hyponatremia *with* weight loss) is described** (per
>   Hew-Butler et al. (2017)). Body mass is a useful but **imperfect**
>   discriminator.
> - **Female sex as an independent risk factor is contested.** The swim data
>   show a 4.5× female excess (Wagner 2012), but the Boston Marathon sex
>   difference **disappeared** after adjusting for BMI and racing time (per
>   Hew-Butler et al. (2017)) — sex may be a proxy for body size and time on
>   course. Honest position: being female is **at minimum not protective**.
> - **Warm water cuts the other way, and the file must not over-rotate into
>   fluid restriction.** Warm-water open-water swimming imposes real thermal
>   strain and real sweat losses (per Chalmers et al. (2021)), unlike pool
>   swimming, where sweat rates are low (per Cox et al. (2002)). Greece in
>   September is a warm-water event. The posture is **measure, don't
>   guess** — not "drink less."
> `[EVIDENCE: swim]` / `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` as marked
> Confidence: medium

> **Swimming is not running, because immersion itself changes fluid
> regulation.** Head-out immersion shifts ~700 mL of blood centrally,
> suppressing vasopressin and driving a diuresis and natriuresis (per
> Epstein (1992)). Practical consequence: **she urinates during the swim, so
> post-swim body-mass loss overestimates sweat loss** — including in
> published swim "sweat rate" figures derived from body mass.
> `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (human immersion physiology)
> Confidence: high for the diuresis; **the further inference that
> post-exit vasopressin rebound explains her low urine output is
> `Coach judgment:` and low confidence — do not present it as evidence.**
> Test: have her void immediately before the post-swim weigh-in, so
> immersion-driven urine loss isn't miscounted as a sweat deficit she then
> "replaces."

perceptual/pacing template that then holds the ceiling in place
independently. Same 90-minute mark, two mechanisms, one root cause.

**Practical cross-link to propose in both files:** before running the
elaborate duration-ceiling probes in §3, establish her habitual

## 8. Gaps — what the literature genuinely does not answer

1. **There is no swim-specific glycogen-depletion or duration-ceiling
   literature at all.** Every glycogen-timeline study cited here is cycling
   (or running), male, lab-based, and 30–60 years old. Swimming's different
   muscle recruitment, horizontal posture, thermal environment and immersion
   physiology are all uncontrolled for. The `[ADAPTED]` tags are doing
   heavy lifting.
2. **Nothing on masters/older female endurance athletes' substrate use.**
   Tarnopolsky (2008) covers sex; the interaction with age (and menopausal
   status, which changes the estradiol mechanism he identifies as causal) is
   not addressed by anything found here. For a **masters female**, this is a
   real hole.
3. **No study measured the performance cost of feed stops in swimming.** The
   20–30 min / 15–20 s cadence is pure practitioner convention. Nobody has
   compared, say, a 20-minute vs. 45-minute feed cadence over a marathon
   swim for either performance or GI tolerance. The time-cost arithmetic in
   this dossier is arithmetic, not evidence.
4. **The optimal carbohydrate *concentration* for a swim feed is unstudied.**
   The tension identified in §1 (high g/h either needs high concentration →
   GI risk, or high fluid volume → EAH risk) is a real and important
   trade-off with **no data behind it**. This is arguably the most useful
   study nobody has run for this sport.
5. **EAH incidence in *warm-water* ultra swims is unknown.** Wagner (2012)
   is a cool Swiss lake; Rogers (2015) is a late-summer ocean swim. Whether
   warm water raises risk (more drinking, more thermal strain) or lowers it
   (genuinely higher sweat losses to absorb the fluid) is **not answered**.
   Her event is warm-water. This gap sits directly on top of her race.
6. **No evidence on how to *interpret* the specific low-urine-output +
   persistent-thirst pattern in the field**, short of a blood test. The
   literature is clear that the symptoms are ambiguous and that body mass
   helps but is imperfect (hypovolemic EAH exists). There is **no validated
   field heuristic**. This is precisely why the library's answer must be
   "get a serum sodium," and why any coaching heuristic offered here would
   be an invention.
7. **Post-exercise rehydration protocols have never been validated after an
   ultra-distance swim.** The 150%/40–60 mmol/L protocol comes from ~2%
   body-mass deficits induced by cycling in the heat, in small groups of
   men, over ~4–6 h. A 4-hour open-water swim with a fluid balance
   confounded by immersion diuresis and hours of hypotonic feeding is a
   materially different problem. The protocol is the best available; it is
   not evidence *for this situation*.
8. **Whether a "trained duration ceiling" can be deliberately raised, and
   how fast.** The expectation/deception studies show a ceiling *exists* and
   is manipulable within a single session. Nothing found addresses the
   training question the athlete actually cares about: how do you raise it
   durably, and over what timeframe? `06-long-swim-progression.md`'s ladder
   is the current answer, and it rests on `Coach judgment:`, not evidence.
