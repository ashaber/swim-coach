## 1. Executive summary

- **Sleep is the highest-leverage, best-evidenced lever available in an
  8-day window.** Mah et al. (2011) — sleep extension to ~10h/night improved
  sprint speed and shooting accuracy in athletes within 5-7 weeks, but even
  short-horizon prioritization of sleep quantity/quality is the
  best-supported recommendation across the literature (Bonnar et al. 2018
  systematic review: sleep extension is the most consistently beneficial
  sleep intervention; napping/hygiene = mixed). Confidence: high (`[ADAPTED:
  general-endurance]`, not swim-specific).
- **Carbohydrate refeeding in the first 1-2h post-effort matters for an
  8-day turnaround**, especially after a 10k swim + 5h kayak (large combined
  glycogen deficit). Ivy et al. (1988) — immediate carbohydrate ingestion
  roughly doubles the rate of glycogen resynthesis vs. a 2h-delayed start.
  Burke et al. (2011) review backs ~1.0-1.2 g/kg/h in the first 4h.
  Confidence: high, `[ADAPTED: cycling]` (classic protocol is cycling, but
  widely generalized).
- **Protein: ~1.65-1.83 g/kg/day (EAR/RDA) supports endurance-athlete
  recovery**, per Kato et al. (2016) — this is the paper the repo's
  `reference_list.md` currently has as "~ not individually verified"; it
  **is now individually verified** (see Topic 2) and its actual numbers can
  replace the placeholder. Koopman et al. (2004) additionally shows
  co-ingesting protein with carbohydrate during a very long (6h) effort
  improves net protein balance during and after — directly relevant to the
  5h-kayak-plus-swim day. Confidence: high, `[EVIDENCE: swim]`-adjacent
  (Kato's subjects were endurance-trained runners, not swimmers —
  `[ADAPTED: running]`).
- **Cold water immersion: genuinely mixed for this exact use case.** Moore
  et al.'s (2022) meta-analysis found CWI **did not** reliably improve
  recovery of *endurance* performance at 24-48h post-exercise, even though
  it helped perceived soreness/recovery and power-based measures. For an
  athlete whose limiter 8 days out is aerobic/endurance capacity (not
  power), CWI is a soreness/comfort tool, not a proven performance-recovery
  accelerant. Confidence: medium, `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`.
- **HRV-guided load adjustment has real RCT support** (Kiviniemi et al.
  2007; Javaloyes et al. 2020; Vesterinen et al. 2016) but all three are in
  runners/cyclists over multi-week blocks, not an 8-day single-athlete
  window — the *mechanism* (down-regulate intensity on a suppressed/
  declining-trend HRV day) is well-evidenced and cheap to apply to the 8-day
  taper-in window even though no study tested exactly this scenario.
  Confidence: medium, `[ADAPTED: running/cycling]`.
- **Subjective wellness self-report is at least as informative as objective
  measures**, per Saw, Main & Gastin (2016) — directly supports leaning on
  the existing `wellness_composite` (subjective 1-5 scale) in `load.py` as
  a legitimate, evidence-backed primary signal rather than a lesser
  substitute for HRV/biomarkers. Confidence: high, `[ADAPTED:
  general-endurance/multi-sport]` (56-study review, not swim-specific).
- **Short (≤7-day) tapers are effective, though 8-14-day tapers show the
  largest gains** — Wang et al. (2023) meta-analysis, 14 studies, ≤21-day
  taper with 41-60% volume cut (intensity/frequency roughly maintained) is
  the general prescription; **shorter tapers (≤7 days) still produced a
  positive effect**, which is the closest evidence-based grounding for an
  8-day gap before the second 10k. Mujika & Padilla's (2003) foundational
  review sets the same volume-cut range (60-90%) and confirms intensity
  should be preserved, not cut. Confidence: medium-high, `[ADAPTED:
  general-endurance]` (not swim-specific, though Mujika's broader body of
  work is swimming-heavy).
- **Swimming-specific recovery-monitoring evidence is thin but exists.**
  Collette et al. (2018) tracked elite swimmers' recovery-stress state
  against training load and found session-RPE (especially distance-weighted
  sRPE) tracked recovery-stress better than ACWR, and that individual
  baselines matter more than group norms — supports the engine's existing
  preference for individualized wellness/load thresholds over one-size ACWR
  cutoffs. `[EVIDENCE: swim]`. Confidence: high (small n=5, but purpose-built
  for this exact population/question).
- **No direct evidence found on swim-paddling/kayak cross-fatigue
  interaction** or on recovery specifically *between two ultra-distance
  open-water swims 8 days apart** — this remains a coach-judgment gap; the
  above sources are the best available adjacent grounding, not direct
  proof.
- **Net recommendation for the 8-day window:** prioritize sleep quantity
  (Mah/Bonnar), front-load carbohydrate + moderate protein in the first
  hours after the kayak/swim day (Ivy/Burke/Kato/Koopman), treat this week
  as a short taper-in rather than a training week (Wang/Mujika &
  Padilla — volume down, intensity preserved, frequency ~unchanged), use
  daily subjective wellness as the primary go/no-go signal (Saw et al.),
  and treat CWI/massage/compression as comfort-tier, not
  performance-recovery-tier, tools (Moore et al.; Driller & Leabeater;
  Hill et al.).

## 2. Verified sources by topic

### Topic 1 — Short-horizon recovery between endurance events (~5-10 days)

- **✓ Driller M., Leabeater A. (2023)** — "Fundamentals or Icing on Top of
  the Cake? A Narrative Review of Recovery Strategies and Devices for
  Athletes" — *Sports (Basel)*, 11(11):213.
  **Verification:** fetched via publisher (MDPI-hosted, PMC-mirrored)
  page; title/authors/journal/year/pages confirmed directly.
  **Summary:** Narrative review classifying recovery modalities (foam
  rolling, compression, cryotherapy, photobiomodulation, hydrotherapy,
  active recovery) by evidence strength. Concludes sleep, nutrition, and
  periodization are the evidence-backed foundation; many newer recovery
  *devices* lack strong research support and shouldn't be prioritized over
  fundamentals.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium (narrative,
  not systematic, review). **Test:** none proposed — descriptive/framing
  source, not a numeric claim to falsify.
  **Grounds:** framing for a future `10-recovery-hrv.md` "fundamentals vs.
  devices" section; no engine constant.

- **✓ Moore E., Fuller J.T., Buckley J.D., Saunders S., Halson S.L.,
  Broatch J.R., Bellenger C.R. (2022)** — "Impact of Cold-Water Immersion
  Compared with Passive Recovery Following a Single Bout of Strenuous
  Exercise on Athletic Performance in Physically Active Participants: A
  Systematic Review with Meta-analysis and Meta-regression" — *Sports
  Medicine*, 52(7):1667-1688.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full-text page directly; title/authors/
  journal/year/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Meta-analysis found CWI improved muscular power recovery
  after eccentric/high-intensity exercise and reduced soreness/CK and
  improved perceived recovery, but was **not effective at improving
  recovery of endurance performance** at 24h or 48h after high-intensity
  exercise. Shorter immersion durations showed a better dose-response than
  longer ones for the outcomes it did help.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium-high (this is
  a meta-analysis, but "strenuous exercise" pools mixed exercise types, not
  ultra-swim-specific). **Test:** if CWI is offered as an intervention in
  the 8-day window, track whether the athlete's own perceived-soreness
  wellness sub-score improves post-CWI without a corresponding change in
  session pace/RPE at matched effort — if pace/RPE doesn't track with CWI
  use, treat it as comfort-only, consistent with this paper's endurance
  null-finding.
  **Grounds:** a future recovery-modality guidance section; explicitly
  *against* over-promising CWI as an endurance-recovery accelerant.

- **✓ Hill J., Howatson G., van Someren K., Leeder J., Pedlar C. (2014)**
  — "Compression garments and recovery from exercise-induced muscle
  damage: A meta-analysis" — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*,
  48:1340-1346.
  **Verification:** title/author/journal/year/pages independently
  corroborated across multiple listings (publisher citation record,
  ResearchGate, Semantic Scholar-style aggregator) — not individually
  fetched full-text, but consistent enough across independent sources to
  treat as ✓ per the legend's "verified real by title/author search."
  **Summary:** Meta-analysis on compression garments and recovery from
  exercise-induced muscle damage; general finding (per repeated secondary
  summaries) is small-to-moderate benefit for soreness/strength recovery,
  strongest in the 24h+ window, weaker/inconsistent for objective
  performance measures.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: low-medium (marker
  reflects not having read the primary abstract directly).
  **Grounds:** comfort-tier recovery-modality guidance, same section as
  Moore et al. above.

- **✓ Braun-Trocchio R., Graybeal A.J., Kreutzer A., Warfield E.,
  Renteria J., Harrison K., Williams A., Moss K., Shah M. (2022)** —
  "Recovery Strategies in Endurance Athletes" — *Journal of Functional
  Morphology and Kinesiology*, 7(1):22.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full-text page directly; title/authors/
  journal/year/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** Survey (not an efficacy trial) of 264 endurance athletes
  across 11 sports on which recovery strategies they use and rate

  **Tag:** `Coach judgment`-adjacent — this is descriptive (what athletes
  *do*), not efficacy evidence; do **not** cite it as proof any practice
  works, only as color for "sleep/nutrition/hydration are what
  practitioners already prioritize."
  **Grounds:** framing text only; no engine constant.

### Topic 2 — Post-exercise nutrition for recovery

- **✓ Kato H., Suzuki K., Bannai M., Moore D.R. (2016)** — "Protein
  Requirements Are Elevated in Endurance Athletes after Exercise as
  Determined by the Indicator Amino Acid Oxidation Method" — *PLOS ONE*,
  11(6):e0157406.
  **Verification:** fetched publisher (PLOS) page directly; confirmed
  exact title, authors, journal, volume/article number, and both headline
  numbers (EAR/RDA).
  **This upgrades `reference_list.md`'s existing "~ Kato H. et al." entry
  to ✓** — the paper is real, individually verified, and its actual
  figures can replace the current unsourced placeholder.
  **Summary:** Six male endurance-trained runners did a 3-day controlled
  training protocol (35 km total) + a 20 km treadmill run, then consumed
  test diets at 0.2-2.8 g/kg/day protein while researchers measured
  phenylalanine oxidation (indicator amino acid oxidation method) to
  derive requirement. Found Estimated Average Requirement = 1.65 g/kg/day,
  Recommended (safe) intake = 1.83 g/kg/day — notably higher than general
  population RDA (0.8 g/kg/day).
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]` (subjects were runners, not swimmers),
  Confidence: high. **Test:** track whether the athlete's self-reported
  recovery/soreness in the 8-day window differs on days protein intake is
  above vs. below ~1.6 g/kg — exploratory, not powered, but checkable
  against her own logs.
  **Grounds:** could ground a future protein-intake-range constant/guidance
  in a nutrition-adjacent library file; currently no engine constant for
  protein g/kg exists to cite it against.

- **✓ Koopman R., Pannemans D.L.E., Jeukendrup A.E., Gijsen A.P.,
  Senden J.M.G., Halliday D., Saris W.H.M., van Loon L.J.C.,
  Wagenmakers A.J.M. (2004)** — "Combined ingestion of protein and
  carbohydrate improves protein balance during ultra-endurance exercise"
  — *American Journal of Physiology-Endocrinology and Metabolism*,
  287(4):E712-720.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed abstract page directly; confirmed
  title, full author list, journal, year, volume/pages.
  **Summary:** Eight endurance-trained athletes did 6h of exercise at 50%
  VO2max (cycle/run/cycle) drinking either carbohydrate-only
  (0.7 g/kg/h) or carbohydrate+protein (0.7 g/kg/h CHO + 0.25 g/kg/h
  protein) every 30 min. Carbohydrate-only kept whole-body protein balance
  negative throughout; adding protein produced a positive or
  less-negative balance during and after exercise.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (mixed-modality lab protocol, not
  swimming), Confidence: medium-high. **Test:** on very long combined-
  effort days (e.g. the swim+kayak day), offer a carb+protein-containing
  feed during the effort rather than carb-only, and log whether next-day
  soreness/wellness composite differs from carb-only long days in her own
  history.
  **Grounds:** future ultra-feeding topic file (`INDEX.md` already flags
  this file as not-yet-authored); directly relevant to fueling the
  swim+kayak combination day itself, not just post-hoc recovery.

- **✓ Ivy J.L. et al. (1988)** — "Muscle glycogen synthesis after
  exercise: effect of time of carbohydrate ingestion" — *Journal of
  Applied Physiology*, 64(4):1480-1485.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed/search cross-check; confirmed
  title, journal, year, volume/pages, and the core numeric finding.
  **Summary:** 12 male cyclists did 70 min of intermittent cycling, then
  ingested a 2 g/kg carbohydrate solution either immediately or 2h
  post-exercise. Immediate ingestion produced roughly double the glycogen
  resynthesis rate (6-8 mmol/kg wet weight/h) vs. delayed ingestion, over
  the first 4h of recovery.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: high (classic, widely
  replicated finding). **Test:** on the day after a long swim/kayak
  effort, confirm carbohydrate intake started within ~30-60 min rather
  than delayed; check whether the athlete's next-day sRPE for an easy
  session is lower (better) on early-refeed days vs. delayed-refeed days
  in her own log history.
  **Grounds:** future ultra-feeding/recovery topic file; no current engine
  constant to cite it against (engine has no post-exercise feeding-timing
  parameter yet).

- **✓ Burke L.M., Hawley J.A., Wong S.H.S., Jeukendrup A.E. (2011)** —
  "Carbohydrates for training and competition" — *Journal of Sports
  Sciences*, 29(sup1):S17-S27.
  **Verification:** title/author/journal/year/pages consistent across
  multiple independent citation listings (university repository, journal
  page, multiple citation-index mirrors).
  **Summary:** IOC-conference-linked review recommending ~1.0-1.2 g/kg/h
  carbohydrate in the first 4h post-exercise for rapid glycogen
  resynthesis, with intake highest-priority in the first 1-2h; general
  carbohydrate-periodization guidance for training vs. competition days.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: high (review by
  leading sports-nutrition authors, widely cited).
  **Grounds:** same future ultra-feeding file as above; would sit
  alongside the existing Shaw (2014)/Jeukendrup (2014)/Martinez-Sanz
  (2024) entries already in `reference_list.md`'s nutrition section as a
  recovery-specific (not during-exercise) carbohydrate citation.

### Topic 3 — Sleep and recovery in athletes

- **✓ Mah C.D., Mah K.E., Kezirian E.J., Dement W.C. (2011)** — "The
  Effects of Sleep Extension on the Athletic Performance of Collegiate
  Basketball Players" — *Sleep*, 34(7):943-950.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed abstract page directly; confirmed
  title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages.
  **Summary:** 11 Stanford basketball players extended sleep toward a
  10h/night minimum for 5-7 weeks after a 2-4 week baseline. Sprint speed
  improved (16.2s -> 15.5s on a timed sprint), free-throw and 3-point
  shooting accuracy each improved ~9%, reaction time improved, and
  players reported better mood/reduced fatigue.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (basketball, not swimming or
  endurance per se — but a foundational, frequently-cited sleep-extension
  RCT-style study), Confidence: medium (single small-n team-sport study;
  the *direction* of effect is corroborated by Bonnar's broader review
  below).
  **Test:** in the 8-day window, if sleep can be extended toward
  8.5-9.5h/night, check whether the athlete's subjective wellness
  composite (energy/soreness sub-scores) trends upward relative to her
  own recent baseline.
  **Grounds:** a future sleep-guidance section in `10-recovery-hrv.md`; no
  current engine constant (the `Wellness` model captures sleep as an input
  to `wellness_composite` but has no sleep-duration target/threshold to
  cite this against yet).

- **✓ Bonnar D., Bartel K., Kakoschke N., et al. (2018)** — "Sleep
  Interventions Designed to Improve Athletic Performance and Recovery: A
  Systematic Review of Current Approaches" — *Sports Medicine*,
  48(3):683-703.
  **Verification:** fetched/cross-checked via publisher page and PubMed
  listing; title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages all confirmed
  consistently.
  **Summary:** Systematic review of sleep interventions in athletes.
  Found sleep **extension** was the most consistently beneficial
  intervention for performance/recovery outcomes, while napping, sleep
  hygiene education, and other post-exercise sleep strategies produced
  mixed results across studies.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: high (systematic
  review, not a single small study). **Test:** same as Mah (2011) above —
  prioritize sleep extension over napping/hygiene-only interventions in
  the 8-day window if a choice must be made.
  **Grounds:** same future sleep-guidance section; this is the stronger
  (review-level) citation to lead with, with Mah (2011) as the illustrative
  underlying study.

### Topic 4 — HRV- and wellness-guided training

- **✓ Kiviniemi A.M. et al. (2007)** — "Endurance training guided
  individually by daily heart rate variability measurements" —
  *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, 101(6):743-751.
  **Verification:** fetched/cross-checked via Springer publisher page and
  independent secondary listings; title, authors, journal, year, and
  volume/pages consistent across sources.
  **Summary:** 26 moderately-fit males randomized to predefined training,
  HRV-guided training, or control, over 4 weeks. HRV-guided group trained
  hard on days with stable/rising morning HRV and easy/rest on days with
  HRV below a 10-day rolling mean-minus-SD or a 2-day declining trend.
  HRV-guided training significantly improved maximal running velocity
  more than the predefined-program group.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]`, Confidence: medium (small n, 4-week
  study; foundational/frequently-replicated protocol design though).
  **Test:** apply the same "hard day only if HRV stable-or-rising, else
  easy/rest" rule inside the 8-day taper-in window and check whether
  wellness-composite trend improves vs. a fixed schedule, using the
  athlete's own trailing-window data as baseline.
  **Grounds:** could ground a future HRV-based daily-adjustment rule
  layered on top of the existing subjective `wellness_composite` /
  `WELLNESS_RED_THRESHOLD` logic in `adapt.py` and `03-periodization.md`
  — note the athlete doesn't currently have an HRV device confirmed in
  her profile; this is forward-looking grounding, not an active engine
  constant today.

- **✓ Vesterinen V., Nummela A., Heikura I., Laine T., Hynynen E.,
  Botella J., Häkkinen K. (2016)** — "Individual Endurance Training
  Prescription with Heart Rate Variability" — *Medicine & Science in
  Sports & Exercise*, 48(7):1347-1354.
  **Verification:** fetched/cross-checked via ACSM/Ovid publisher listing
  and PubMed; title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages confirmed.
  **Summary:** 40 recreational endurance runners split into an HRV-guided
  group vs. a traditional predefined-program group over an 8-week
  intensive block (after a 4-week prep phase). HRV-guided training
  produced greater aerobic-performance gains than the fixed program,
  attributed to concentrating hard/high-intensity sessions on days of
  good recovery.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: running]`, Confidence: medium (RCT, moderate n, but
  running not swimming). **Test:** same as Kiviniemi (2007) above.
  **Grounds:** same future HRV-adjustment rule as Kiviniemi (2007); the
  two together form a reasonably solid `[ADAPTED]` pair (independent labs,
  same directional finding).

- **✓ Javaloyes A., Sarabia J.M., Lamberts R.P., Plews D.,
  Moya-Ramon M. (2020)** — "Training Prescription Guided by Heart Rate
  Variability Vs. Block Periodization in Well-Trained Cyclists" —
  *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*, 34(6):1511-1518.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed abstract page directly; confirmed
  title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages.
  **Summary:** 20 well-trained cyclists, 8 weeks, HRV-guided vs. block
  periodization. HRV-guided group improved VO2max, peak power output,
  ventilatory thresholds, and 40-min time-trial performance more than the
  block-periodization group, which showed only one significant
  improvement across the same measures.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: cycling]`, Confidence: medium (single-lab RCT,
  well-trained but not elite/swim population). **Test:** same HRV-day-type
  logic as above, cross-discipline triangulation now spans
  running/running/cycling (Kiviniemi, Vesterinen, Javaloyes).
  **Grounds:** same future HRV-adjustment section.

- **✓ Saw A.E., Main L.C., Gastin P.B. (2016)** — "Monitoring the athlete
  training response: subjective self-reported measures trump commonly
  used objective measures: a systematic review" — *British Journal of
  Sports Medicine*, 50(5):281-291.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed abstract page directly; confirmed
  title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages.
  **Summary:** Systematic review of 56 studies comparing subjective
  (self-report) vs. objective (physiological/biochemical/performance)
  measures for monitoring athlete training response. Found subjective and
  objective measures generally did not correlate with each other, and
  subjective measures showed **greater sensitivity and consistency** in
  detecting both acute and chronic training-load responses than the
  objective measures tested.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance/multi-sport]`, Confidence: high
  (large systematic review, though not swim-specific).
  **Test:** none additional needed — this directly supports treating the
  existing `wellness_composite` (subjective, 1-5 scale) as a
  first-class/primary signal, not a fallback for when HRV/objective data
  is unavailable.
  **Grounds:** directly grounds the existing `WELLNESS_RED_THRESHOLD` /
  `wellness_composite` design in `engine/swim_coach/load.py` and
  `adapt.py`, and `library/03-periodization.md`'s wellness-composite
  section — this is the single strongest citation found in this pass for
  an *already-implemented* engine constant, and should probably be added
  to `reference_list.md`'s wellness/monitoring area regardless of whether
  `10-recovery-hrv.md` is authored yet.

### Topic 5 — Swimming-specific recovery

- **✓ Collette R., Kellmann M., Ferrauti A., Meyer T., Pfeiffer M.
  (2018)** — "Relation Between Training Load and Recovery-Stress State in
  High-Performance Swimming" — *Frontiers in Physiology*, 9:845.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full-text page directly; confirmed title,
  authors, journal, year, article number.
  **Summary:** Monitored 5 elite female swimmers over 17 weeks using
  session-RPE load metrics and the Acute Recovery and Stress Scale
  (ARSS). Found substantial individual variation in how training load
  translated to recovery-stress state; sRPE (particularly a
  distance-weighted variant) related to recovery-stress state more
  strongly than acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR); recommended
  individual baselines over group-level thresholds.
  **Tag:** `[EVIDENCE: swim]`, Confidence: high for the population/
  question match (this is swimmers, and it's about training load vs.
  recovery-stress specifically), though n=5 is small.
  **Test:** none additional — this is corroborating evidence for a
  decision the engine already makes.
  **Grounds:** directly supports `03-periodization.md`'s existing note
  that ACWR is a "weak predictor"/"criticized methodology" and that
  sRPE/individual-baseline monitoring should be preferred — this paper is
  swim-specific corroboration for a claim that file currently sources
  from the (running) Garmin-RunSafe cohort. Should likely be added
  alongside that citation.
  I found **no other swim-specific recovery evidence** — nothing on
  swim/paddling cross-fatigue interaction, and no swim-specific shoulder-
  load-between-hard-sessions RCT beyond what `07-strength-dryland.md`
  already cites (Feijen et al. 2021, already in `reference_list.md`).

### Topic 6 — Mini-taper for a B-event ~1 week out

- **✓ Mujika I., Padilla S. (2003)** — "Scientific bases for
  precompetition tapering strategies" — *Medicine & Science in Sports &
  Exercise*, 35(7):1182-1187.
  **Verification:** fetched via PubMed abstract page directly; confirmed
  title, authors, journal, year, volume/pages.
  **Summary:** Foundational review of taper physiology. Recommends
  maintaining training intensity while reducing volume 60-90% and
  frequency by no more than ~20%; taper duration 4->28 days studied, with
  progressive non-linear tapers outperforming step tapers; typical
  performance gain ~3% (range 0.5-6.0%) from cardiorespiratory, metabolic,
  hormonal, neuromuscular, and psychological adaptations.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` (Mujika's broader body of work
  is swimming-heavy, but this specific review is cross-discipline, not
  swim-specific), Confidence: high (the canonical taper review, already
  named as "the canonical taper author" in the repo's existing Formosa
  entry).
  **Grounds:** the primary citation this project has been missing for
  taper *mechanism* (as opposed to Formosa's single-case *example*);
  should sit next to the corrected Formosa entry in
  `reference_list.md`.

- **✓ Wang Z., Wang Y.T., Gao W., Zhong Y. (2023)** — "Effects of
  tapering on performance in endurance athletes: A systematic review and
  meta-analysis" — *PLOS ONE*, 18(5):e0282838.
  **Verification:** fetched PMC full-text page directly; confirmed title,
  authors, journal, year, article number.
  **Summary:** Meta-analysis of 14 studies. Tapering significantly
  improved time-trial/time-to-exhaustion performance (no significant
  effect on VO2max or movement economy). A taper of ≤21 days with 41-60%
  volume reduction (intensity/frequency roughly held) was generally
  effective; **tapers of ≤7 days still produced a positive effect**,
  though the 8-14-day duration band showed the largest gains overall;
  pairing taper with prior planned overload improved results further.
  **Tag:** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`, Confidence: medium-high
  (meta-analysis; not swim-specific, and doesn't isolate a "B-event mini-
  taper 8 days before an A-event" scenario specifically — it studies
  taper-before-the-only-event designs).
  **Test:** for the athlete's 8-day gap, this supports a volume cut (not
  as large as a full 41-60%, since 8 days is short and this is a B/A
  event pairing rather than a single peak) while holding intensity and
  session frequency close to normal; track whether her post-event-2
  perceived-freshness/RPE-at-pace differs from a matched no-taper
  historical week, if one exists in her logs.
  **Grounds:** this is the most directly useful new source in the whole
  pass for a **future between-events mini-taper rule** — it's the closest
  evidence found to the exact "second hard event ~1 week later" shape of
  the immediate use case, even though it's evidence about tapering into a
  single event rather than specifically about back-to-back events.

## 4. Proposed outline for `library/10-recovery-hrv.md`

Per `00-conventions.md`: keep to ≤~2,500 words, tag every claim, mark
`UNREVIEWED` until human-reviewed, cite `reference_list.md` entries by
title+author, and match existing topic-file style (see
`03-periodization.md` / `06-long-swim-progression.md` for tone/format).

1. **Recovery windows between hard endurance efforts (5-10 days)**
   - Framing: this file's primary trigger scenario (a combined
     swim+paddle day followed by a second hard event ~1 week later).
   - Sources: Driller & Leabeater (2023) for the fundamentals-vs-devices
     frame; Braun-Trocchio et al. (2022) for what athletes actually
     prioritize (sleep/nutrition/hydration).
   - Engine tie-in: none directly yet — candidate future constant, a
     "short-turnaround" recovery-day minimum distinct from
     `RECOVERY_DAYS_AFTER_MILESTONE_MIN/MAX` in `adapt.py` (those are
     tuned for the long-swim ladder, not a fixed B-to-A-event gap).

2. **Nutrition for the refeed window**
   - Carbohydrate timing/dose: Ivy et al. (1988), Burke et al. (2011).
   - Protein dose: Kato et al. (2016) — replaces the current unsourced
     `~ Kato H. et al.` placeholder in `reference_list.md` with the real
     EAR/RDA figures.
   - Combined-effort-day fueling (relevant to the swim+kayak day itself,
     not just after): Koopman et al. (2004).
   - Engine tie-in: none yet; this section would be the natural home for
     a future protein-g/kg-per-day guidance constant if the engine adds
     one.

3. **Sleep**
   - Bonnar et al. (2018) systematic review as the lead citation; Mah et
     al. (2011) as the illustrative underlying study.
   - Engine tie-in: `Wellness` model already captures sleep as an input
     to `wellness_composite` (`load.py`) — this section would document
     *why* sleep gets weight in that composite, without yet adding a
     standalone sleep-duration threshold.

4. **Cold water immersion, massage, compression — comfort vs. performance
   tiers**
   - Moore et al. (2022) as the central "doesn't move endurance-specific
     recovery" caveat; Hill et al. (2014) for compression; Driller &
     Leabeater (2023) again for the overall device-evidence framing.
   - Explicitly warn against over-promising CWI as an endurance-recovery
     accelerant for the 8-day scenario — soreness/comfort benefit only.

5. **HRV- and wellness-guided load adjustment**
   - Lead with Saw, Main & Gastin (2016) to justify the engine's existing
     subjective-first approach (`WELLNESS_RED_THRESHOLD`,
     `wellness_composite`, `WELLNESS_WINDOW_DAYS` in `adapt.py`/
     `load.py`).
   - Then Kiviniemi (2007), Vesterinen (2016), Javaloyes (2020) as the
     `[ADAPTED: running/cycling]` evidence base for an HRV-day-type rule,
     clearly marked as forward-looking (no HRV device currently in the
     athlete's profile) rather than describing an active engine constant.
   - Engine tie-in: directly grounds existing `WELLNESS_RED_THRESHOLD` /
     `wellness_composite`; proposes (not yet implements) an HRV-based
     daily-adjustment layer.

6. **Swimming-specific recovery-load monitoring**
   - Collette et al. (2018) — sRPE over ACWR, individual baselines over
     group norms, in elite swimmers specifically.
   - Engine tie-in: corroborates (with swim-specific data) the
     ACWR-is-weak caveat already in `03-periodization.md`, currently
     sourced only from the running Garmin-RunSafe cohort.

7. **Mini-taper for a B-event ~1 week out**
   - Mujika & Padilla (2003) for taper mechanism/parameters; Wang et al.
     (2023) for the short-taper (≤7-day) specific finding closest to this
     scenario; cross-reference the existing (corrected) Formosa entry in
     `reference_list.md` for the single swim-specific case study.
   - Engine tie-in: candidate new constant — a `MINI_TAPER_*` rule
     distinct from the macro-block taper `03-periodization.md` already
     describes, since 8 days is shorter than any macro-taper the engine
     currently models. Flag explicitly as **not yet implemented** and
     `UNREVIEWED` until a human decides the actual volume-cut number for
     this specific short window.
