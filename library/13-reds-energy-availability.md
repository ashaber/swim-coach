# RED-S / chronic energy availability

**UNREVIEWED** — drafted from
`library/research-dossiers/2026-07-13-reds-energy-availability.md` (sources

The athlete raised RED-S herself and will read this file. Covers **chronic**
energy availability (EA) — habitual intake vs. expenditure and its
endocrine/bone/performance consequences — as distinct from
`08-ultra-feeding.md`, which owns acute in-session grams/hour and mechanics.
They interlock at one point: chronic low EA (LEA) is a plausible upstream
cause of `08`'s 90-minute wall, and a well-executed acute-fuelling fix would
**not** be expected to resolve it (see below). Also cross-links
`10-recovery-hrv.md` (an HRV/RHR confound) and `07-strength-dryland.md`
(the one actionable, swim-specific training move here).

**Scope line, stated once, enforced throughout:** this system can flag risk
patterns and recommend evaluation. It cannot diagnose RED-S/REDs, cannot
prescribe treatment, cannot interpret blood work or bone density, and must
never talk anyone out of seeking care. Equally, it must not fear-monger —
the science below is genuinely contested and LEA sits on a spectrum, not a
verdict.

## The model, and what this system will not do

`Coach judgment:` Energy availability (intake minus exercise energy
expenditure, per kg fat-free mass) is the organising concept behind
RED-S/REDs. **This system does not and will not compute an EA number.**
`Burke, Lundy, Fahrenholtz & Melin (2018)` — themselves RED-S consensus
authors — document that field EA estimation has no standard protocol and
carries substantial error in every term; `Jeukendrup et al. (2024)` show a
reported 34% LEA prevalence collapsing to 5% after correcting for
measurement error. Any EA figure this system produced would be noise
dressed as diagnosis.

**The famous "30 kcal/kg FFM/day" threshold is not settled and is not a
target.** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: low-medium. It
originates in `Loucks & Thuma (2003)` — 5 days, 29 **sedentary** young
women, LH pulsatility. `Areta, Taylor & Koehler (2021)` state thresholds
are "debated in females and unknown in males," and **the 2023 IOC consensus
itself abandoned fixed cut-offs** (`Mountjoy et al. (2023)`) for an
adaptable-vs-problematic LEA spectrum. **Test:** none — this claim exists to
*prevent* a test. If any future engine constant or coach reply quotes
"30 kcal/kg" as a number Renee should hit, that is a bug.

**The evidence base is genuinely contested — this must be said plainly, not
smoothed over.** The Female Athlete Triad expert panel formally rebutted the
IOC's own RED-S consensus (`De Souza, Williams, Nattiv et al. (2014)`);
`Jeukendrup et al. (2024)` argue the diagnosis is circular (symptoms define
LEA, but LEA can't be measured, so diagnosis rests on those same generic
symptoms) and propose a multi-stressor checklist instead of a single-cause
energy model. This is a live dispute between senior researchers, not a
fringe objection — a file that presents RED-S as settled would misrepresent
the field.

## Chronic LEA is a plausible upstream cause of the 90-minute wall — and acute fuelling will not fix it

`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: medium-high. `Caldwell et al.
(2024)`: 12 endurance-trained **women**, randomised blinded crossover, 14
days at low (22.3 kcal/kg FFM/day) vs. optimal (51.9) EA — 20-min
time-trial performance impaired **7.8%**, an impairment that **persisted
after 3 days of refuelling**, with muscle glycogen and mitochondrial
capacity **unchanged**. **Test:** (the key one for the 90-minute wall) once
in-session fuelling is corrected per `08-ultra-feeding.md`, track
time-to-RPE-inflection on matched long sessions for 3-4 weeks. If durability
does **not** improve — especially alongside falling body mass, poor sleep,
or repeat illness — that pattern warrants a **sports physician and
registered sports dietitian**, not another training tweak, and the system
should say exactly that rather than offering a workout as a substitute.
"I ate a big dinner and still felt flat" is not evidence against LEA — the
3-day-refuel persistence above is exactly why.

## Swimming is not osteogenic — the highest-value swim-specific finding here

`[EVIDENCE: swim]` Confidence: high. `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2013)`
(systematic review, 64 studies) and `Gómez-Bruton et al. (2018)`
(meta-analysis, 18-30y) both find swimmers' bone mineral density **similar
to sedentary controls and lower than high-impact athletes** — swimming does
not buy the bone protection running/jumping does, even in well-fuelled
swimmers. Stacked with post-menopausal oestrogen withdrawal and any
LEA-driven suppression of bone formation, that is **three independent hits
on the same tissue, a combination no study examines.** This is a clinical
question — a baseline DXA and a sports-physician conversation is the
concrete first step, not something this system estimates around.
`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: medium. `Hutson et al. (2021)`
argue high-impact loading is highly osteogenic and energy-cheap, and is
under-used in endurance athletes — a proposal, not a completed trial.
**Test:** adding brief impact/plyometric work to the existing 2x/week
dryland block (`07-strength-dryland.md`) should cost near-zero extra
session RPE or duration and should not aggravate any joint complaint in her
wellness log. **This is the one training-side action in this whole file
that is defensible regardless of how the RED-S debate resolves** — swimming
is non-osteogenic no matter her energy status.

## The classic warning sign does not exist for this athlete

`Coach judgment:` (grounded in `Heikura et al. (2018)` and
`Gowers et al. (2025)`). Menstrual dysfunction is the primary indicator the
Triad, LEAF-Q, and most REDs screening were built around; `Heikura et al.
(2018)` found reproductive-hormone function the *most reliable* marker
available. **For a peri-/post-menopausal athlete that marker carries zero
information**, and `Gowers et al. (2025)` — the only cohort found in her
age band (females 45±13y) — explicitly warn peri-menopausal symptoms may
**artificially inflate** LEAF-Q scores (77% of their women flagged "at
risk," but only 9% had low bone density). **This system will not run a
LEAF-Q-style score for her and will not treat its absence as reassurance.**
The honest statement is: there is no validated screening instrument for
your demographic.

**Screening instruments rule out; they do not rule in — and in swimmers
specifically, the standard one failed.** `[EVIDENCE: swim]` Confidence:
medium. `Klein et al. (2023)`: 30 NCAA D-III swimmers, 43% measured below 30
kcal/kg FFM, and LEAF-Q **failed to discriminate** low-EA from adequate-EA
women (caught 3 of 6, false-flagged 3 others). Corroborated at the
instrument level: high sensitivity/negative-predictive-value, **low
specificity** (`Rogers et al. (2021)`) — useful to rule out, useless to
diagnose. **Test:** none — this claim exists to stop the system from
adopting a questionnaire score as a finding.

**RMR ratio is not a validated LEA test.** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`
Confidence: medium. Promising but unstandardised and insensitive to milder
deficiency (`Sterringer & Larson-Meyer (2022)`); `Espinar et al. (2025)`
found **no association** between EA and RMR/RMR-ratio in 49 competitive
athletes. If a clinician orders indirect calorimetry, fine — the app must
not infer metabolic suppression from anything it has. **Test:** none — this
claim exists to stop the system from ever treating an RMR-ratio number as a
finding, the same way the LEAF-Q claim above stops it treating a
questionnaire score as one.

## What this system can legitimately notice

**Within-day energy distribution.** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`
Confidence: medium. In 25 elite female endurance athletes with **matched**
24-hour EA, those with menstrual dysfunction spent more of the day in
deficit — timing, not just the daily total, tracked with dysfunction
(`Fahrenholtz et al. (2018)`). **Test:** on her biggest training days
(top-decile logged load), check whether her first substantial intake
follows the session by a long gap — an early pool practice with the first
real meal hours later is a flaggable habit, without computing any EA number
or implying a diagnosis. Frame it as adding food earlier, never as an audit.

**The swim/cold-water appetite folklore is backwards.**
`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: medium. Water-based exercise does
**not** suppress energy intake vs. land-based exercise and *increases* it
vs. rest; **cold water (18-20°C) increases post-exercise intake
substantially more than neutral water** (`Grigg et al. (2023)`,
meta-analysis of 8 acute studies; `White et al. (2005)` is the classic
underlying trial, n=11 men). `Coach judgment:` post-cold-swim hunger is a
documented physiological response, not a lapse — she may have been blaming
herself for it, and the system should never frame it as something to manage
down. The swim-specific LEA hazard is structural — large expenditure,
sessions where eating mid-swim is mechanically impossible, cold adding to
the bill, early-practice deficit windows — **not appetite-mediated.**
**Test:** none proposed — this claim exists to remove a harmful assumption.

## What restoring energy availability actually looks like — and its limits

`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: low-medium (n=8, uncontrolled).
`Cialdella-Kam et al. (2014)`: **+360 kcal/day for 6 months** restored
menses in 7 of 8 exercise-associated amenorrheic athletes (mean 2.6 months
to resumption), with **little change in bone or muscle outcomes over the
same 6 months**. Two honest readings: the corrective dose can be modest and
unglamorous, and **bone does not bounce back on the same timescale** — the
argument for acting on a suspicion early rather than waiting for a stress
fracture to settle it. **Test:** none — this is a description of what the
literature shows, **not a prescription** — any actual intake target for her
is a registered sports dietitian's call, informed by a sports physician's
assessment
(`Stellingwerff et al. (2023)`, whose REDs CAT2 makes final diagnosis
explicitly **physician-led** — the citation for this system's refusal to
diagnose).

## HRV/RHR confound (cross-reference `10-recovery-hrv.md`)

`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: medium. `Tegg et al. (2024)`
(marked **~**, author list unconfirmed — verify before this claim leaves
`UNREVIEWED`) found physically active women with secondary amenorrhea had
**lower resting heart rate** and lower blood pressure alongside **impaired
endothelial function and worse lipids** — a harmful energy/hormonal state
can present with wearable signals a training system reads as *improving
fitness*. `10-recovery-hrv.md` rates resting heart rate as its
**highest-confidence** Oura signal; that remains true about measurement
*accuracy*, but not about *meaning* — a falling RHR is not self-evidently
good news alongside falling body mass, declining durability, or rising
illness frequency. `Coach judgment:` treat that **divergence** as a flag
rather than netting the two signals against each other. **Test:** if her
Oura RHR trends down while `wellness_composite` and matched-effort pace/RPE
trend the wrong way, surface the divergence explicitly rather than
averaging it away. **HRV under LEA is a genuine unknown** — no primary
study of HRV in energy-deficient trained athletes was verified; this
matters for any future HRV-guided load rule and should not be assumed to
move in a particular direction.

## Gaps, stated bluntly

The masters/peri-/post-menopausal female athlete is close to an evidence
vacuum, and she is standing in it. No validated screening instrument exists
for her demographic. No EA threshold has ever been derived in her
population — the 30 kcal/kg figure is from 5 days in sedentary young women.
The canonical red flag (amenorrhea) is unavailable. The two most-cited
consequence studies exclude her age band entirely (15-30y and 18-40y); the
swim performance study is in 15-17-year-olds; the bone meta-analysis is in
18-30-year-olds. The bone-risk stack (menopause + non-osteogenic sport +
any LEA suppression) is unquantified — no study examines it. No
ultra-distance open-water swimming LEA evidence exists at any age. The
syndrome's own status is contested among senior researchers.

**Net honest statement, carried close to verbatim:** chronic low energy
availability is a real and plausible concern for someone training at this
volume in a sport with no bone protection. It is also a field with
contested science, no validated screening tool for her age group, and no
evidence base in ultra-distance open-water swimmers of any age. This system
can tell her what patterns it sees in her own data; the actual answer has
to come from a sports physician and registered sports dietitian, with a
bone-density scan as the concrete first step. This is not something a
training app should try to settle, in either direction.
