# Ultra feeding: the 90-minute wall, in-swim carbohydrate, and rehydration

**UNREVIEWED** — drafted from `library/research-dossiers/2026-07-13-ultra-feeding.md`
(sources independently spot-verified); pending human review before treated
as grounding truth, per `00-conventions.md`.

Answers the athlete's hottest logged question (asked 3x): a "my body is
done" wall at ~90 minutes that coincides with her habitual pool-session
length. Also covers in-swim carbohydrate feeding and post-swim rehydration,
including an exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) safety section — read
that section before advising anything about her reported low urine output.
See `00-conventions.md` for the tagging scheme and `reference_list.md` for
citations. Cross-links `13-reds-energy-availability.md` (chronic energy
availability) and `06-long-swim-progression.md` (duration progression).

## The 90-minute wall is not a plausible muscle-glycogen wall

**The question's premise is the first thing to correct, honestly.** A
~90-minute "done" point is **not** where trained-athlete muscle-glycogen
depletion classically lands. `[ADAPTED: cycling]` Confidence: high. Time to
exhaustion at 70-75% VO2max on a normal mixed diet ran ~126 min in
`Bergström et al. (1967)` and ~170 min in `Coggan & Coyle (1987)`. Her sex
pushes this later, not earlier: women oxidize proportionally more fat and
less carbohydrate at matched intensity, with evidence of muscle-glycogen
sparing (`Tarnopolsky (2008)`). **Test:** compare her wall's timing across
sessions of clearly different intensity. True substrate depletion tracks
**work done**, so it must arrive later in an easy session; a wall that lands
at ~90 min regardless of intensity is not a glycogen story.

**The glycogen hypothesis survives in exactly one form: low *starting*
glycogen.** `[ADAPTED: cycling]` Confidence: medium-high. In the same
`Bergström et al. (1967)` subjects, time to exhaustion fell to ~59 min on a
carbohydrate-poor diet vs. ~126 min on a mixed diet — a band that brackets a
90-minute wall for an athlete starting sessions under-carbed. **Test:** log
carbohydrate intake in the 24h before each session and whether it was
fasted; if wall timing correlates with pre-session carbohydrate
availability, this is a fuelling problem, not a fixed ceiling. **This is the
one direct link to chronic energy availability — see
`13-reds-energy-availability.md`, whose `Caldwell et al. (2024)` finding is
the fallback hypothesis if fixing acute fuelling doesn't move the wall.**

**A third, overlooked candidate: liver glycogen / blood glucose, not muscle
glycogen — cheap to rule out first.** `[ADAPTED: cycling]` Confidence:
medium. Liver glycogen is a much smaller store than muscle glycogen and is
substantially depleted by an overnight fast (`Gonzalez et al. (2016)`;
cited qualitatively only — specific gram figures were not verified from
primary text, and none should be quoted here). In `Coggan & Coyle (1987)`,
fatigue coincided with plasma glucose falling to 3.1 mmol/L and was
**reversed** by glucose ingestion/infusion — a blood-glucose phenomenon, not
a muscle-glycogen one. If she trains early-morning fasted (common for
coached squads), this fits a 90-minute wall better than muscle depletion
does. **Test:** run one matched session fed (~1 g/kg carbohydrate, 1-2h
prior) vs. one fasted. If the wall moves substantially, this is a cheap fix
— rule it out before any elaborate duration-ceiling intervention.

**Competing explanation: a habituated, perceptual duration ceiling.**
`[ADAPTED: running]` Confidence: medium-high (the finding that expectation
moves RPE is solid; the "central governor" model built on top of it is
**contested**, and should not be presented as settled). RPE is scaled
against a subconscious template keyed to the *expected* session endpoint
(`Tucker (2009)`); manipulating expected duration shifts RPE, affect and
pace with physiological demand held constant (`Baden et al. (2005)`,
corroborated by `Eston et al. (2012)`). A wall that arrives at her habitual
*clock time* regardless of work done is this signature, not an empty tank.
**Test:** tell her in advance a session is 2.5h rather than ~90 min,
changing nothing else. If the wall softens or moves, the ceiling is at
least partly perceptual — then progress duration deliberately via
`06-long-swim-progression.md`'s existing ladder (same +≤15% cap).

**Critical trap: "carbs fixed it" does NOT prove it was glycogen.**
`[ADAPTED: cycling]` Confidence: high. Carbohydrate feeding delays fatigue
**without sparing muscle glycogen** — it works via blood glucose
(`Coyle et al. (1986)`). Separately, a carbohydrate *mouth rinse* — swilled
and spat, zero calories absorbed — improves performance and activates
taste/reward brain regions on fMRI, independent of sweetness
(`Chambers et al. (2009)`). So a feed can push the wall back through a
purely oral/central route with no substrate delivered at all. **Test: the
only interpretable feeding trial includes a non-caloric, sweet-matched
mouth-rinse arm.** Real feed vs. rinse vs. nothing: if rinse ≈ real feed,
the benefit is central, not substrate, and the intervention is a duration
progression, not a nutrition plan. This is a structured coaching probe, not
a blinded experiment — she can't be blinded to fasted/fed or duration
expectation, so treat results as suggestive, not conclusive.

**Most likely answer, as coach judgment, not evidence:** these are not
mutually exclusive. A genuinely low starting glycogen (chronic under-fuelling
and/or fasted training) would lower the physiological ceiling *and* teach
the perceptual template at the same 90-minute mark simultaneously. The
"trained ceiling vs. glycogen" framing is a **false dichotomy** if she is
chronically under-fuelled — see `13-reds-energy-availability.md` before
running the elaborate duration-ceiling probes above; if she is
under-fuelled, that's the cheaper and more urgent explanation.

## In-session carbohydrate feeding

**Dose ladder, and it does not treat 90 minutes as substrate-limited.**
`[EVIDENCE: swim]` for the open-water application, `[ADAPTED: cycling]` for
the transporter physiology. Confidence: high. ~30 g/h for 1-2h efforts,
~60 g/h for 2-3h, up to **90 g/h for >2.5-3h** from multiple-transportable
sources (glucose+fructose or maltodextrin+fructose — single-transportable
carbohydrate saturates gut absorption at ~60 g/h) — per
`Jeukendrup (2014)` and `Shaw et al. (2014)`, both already in
`reference_list.md`. Notably, the literature's own prescription for a
90-minute session is ~30 g/h — it does not treat 90 min as a substrate wall.
**Test:** for the 33.3 km target (~9-12h), the operative band is 60-90 g/h;
verify tolerance on long swims, logging GI symptoms against g/h actually
consumed, not planned.

**90 g/h is a trained capacity, not a starting one.** `[ADAPTED: running]`
Confidence: medium-high. Two weeks of repeated in-session carbohydrate
exposure reduced GI symptoms and malabsorption and improved performance
(`Miall et al. (2018)`); gut absorption and gastric emptying are
nutrient-specifically trainable (`Jeukendrup (2017)`). **COI note:** the
carbohydrate-dose literature originates substantially from industry-funded
and industry-adjacent labs; the findings are well-replicated, but that
context is worth stating. **Test:** rehearse the exact race feed (product,
concentration, cadence) on every long open-water swim, tracking GI symptom
scores across the block; expect tolerance to improve over 2+ weeks.

**Feed cadence costs less time than swimmers assume.** `Coach judgment:`
practitioner convention (channel-swimming associations; no controlled study
measured the performance cost of feed stops in swimming, and the only
survey of Channel swimmers' feeding behaviour could not be verified as
peer-reviewed, so it is not cited as evidence here). A feed every 20-30 min
at 15-20s (bottle on a rope, vertical skulling) costs roughly 40s/hour —
well under 1% of total swim time. The gut and the fluid volume are the real
constraints, not the clock.

**Do not let the carbohydrate target drag a dangerous fluid volume behind
it.** `Coach judgment:` (assembled from `Jeukendrup (2014)` on dose and
`Hew-Butler et al. (2017)` on fluid volume; this specific combination has
not been studied). Confidence: low-medium — flagged as reasoning, not
evidence. Delivering 90 g/h from a dilute (~8%) drink requires ~1.1 L/h; over
a 9-12h swim that is exactly the hypotonic fluid load that causes EAH (see
below). Hit the carbohydrate target with **more concentrated feeds**, not
more fluid. **Test:** compute the litres/hour implied by her planned feed
schedule before the swim; compare against measured body-mass change on long
training swims (nude, post-void, before/after). If planned intake exceeds
measured losses, the plan is wrong.

## Post-swim rehydration

**When a deficit is *measured*, replace ~150% of it over ~4-6h with
~40-60+ mmol/L sodium.** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: high for
the protocol — but every underlying study is small-n, male, cycling-based,
and measures a ~2% deficit, not a multi-hour open-water swim. Sodium is what
keeps fluid in; plain water just increases urine output
(`Shirreffs et al. (1996)`; `Merson et al. (2008)`; `Evans et al. (2017)`).
Most sports drinks (~10-25 mmol/L) are too dilute for this; an oral
rehydration solution or fluid with salty food is the practical route. Spread
intake over hours — a bolus provokes diuresis. **Test:** weigh nude,
post-void, immediately before and after long swims; multiply the deficit by
1.5 for the replacement volume. **If there is no deficit, there is nothing
to replace** — see the safety rail below.

**The 150% rule is conditional on a *measured* deficit — applying it to an
assumed one is the overdrinking pathway into hyponatremia.**
`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: high. Both hypohydration and
**hyperhydration** compromise performance and health
(`McDermott et al. (2017)`). **Test:** treat any fluid volume given without a
matching measured pre/post body-mass deficit as a protocol violation to
flag, not a data point to trust.

## SAFETY RAIL — exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH)

**Low urine output and persistent thirst after a long swim, despite
electrolyte intake, is an ambiguous symptom pattern that includes EAH — and
the intuitive response ("drink more") is the one that can kill. This is a
medical question, requiring a serum sodium measurement, not a coaching
heuristic.**

- **Electrolyte intake does not exclude EAH.** `[ADAPTED: general-endurance]`
  Confidence: high. Sodium ingestion may attenuate the fall in blood sodium
  but **cannot prevent EAH when fluid intake is excessive** — it is fluid
  *volume*, not sodium, that drives the final blood-sodium concentration
  (`Hew-Butler et al. (2017)`; `Hew-Butler et al. (2015)` consensus).
  "But she was taking electrolytes" is not reassurance. **Test:** none — this
  is a safety statement, not a hypothesis to weigh against her training data;
  treat reported electrolyte intake as irrelevant to whether EAH should be
  suspected.
- **Low urine output is a listed EAH sign**, via non-osmotic vasopressin
  secretion causing water retention, alongside headache, nausea, vomiting,
  confusion and weight gain — and it overlaps almost completely with
  dehydration. Giving hypotonic fluid is correct for dehydration and
  **detrimental** for EAH (`Rosner (2019)`).
- **Her population carries the highest documented ultra-swim risk.**
  `[EVIDENCE: swim-ultra]` Confidence: high that the risk is real and
  population-matched; the incidence estimate rests on a single 36-person
  event. In a 26.4 km open-water swim, EAH occurred in **36% of female and
  8% of male** swimmers (`Wagner et al. (2012)`) — the closest population
  match in the literature to this athlete. A woman completing a 20 km
  open-ocean swim presented with **seizures and serum sodium 119 mmol/L**,
  requiring ICU care and hypertonic saline, and recovered fully
  (`Rogers et al. (2015)`, case-report tier — n=1 establishes the failure
  mode, not its incidence; read together with Wagner).

**What the coach does — and does not — do:**
1. Do not tell her to drink more, not as a default, not as a hedge.
2. Weigh nude, post-void, before and after every long swim. Mass **down** →
   a real deficit, rehydrate per the 150% protocol above. Mass **stable or
   up** with low urine output → withhold extra fluid and refer for medical
   evaluation.
3. Escalate immediately — urgent medical care, no fluids — if low urine
   output comes with headache, nausea/vomiting, confusion, disorientation,
   or puffiness.
4. Ask about NSAID use — a documented EAH risk factor, common in masters
   athletes (`Rosner (2019)`).
5. Recurrence is the trigger for a serum sodium test; symptoms alone cannot
   settle this.

**Test:** none. This is a referral rule, not a claim to falsify against her
training data — `00-conventions.md`'s acute-symptom override applies.

**Honest limits, so this section doesn't overclaim.** Persistent thirst
alone leans mildly *against* hyponatremia (osmotic thirst should be
suppressed when plasma is dilute) — but it cannot exclude it, because
volume-driven thirst persists and hypovolemic EAH (hyponatremia *with*
weight loss) is described (`Hew-Butler et al. (2017)`). Body mass is useful
but **imperfect**. Female sex as an independent EAH risk factor is
contested: the swim data show a 4.5x female excess (`Wagner (2012)`), but
the sex difference **disappeared** in Boston Marathon data after adjusting
for BMI and race time (`Hew-Butler et al. (2017)`) — being female is at
minimum not protective, but is not established as independently causal.
**Warm water (Greece) genuinely raises fluid need — do not over-rotate into
restriction.** `[EVIDENCE: swim]` Confidence: medium. Warm-water open-water
racing produces real thermal strain (`Chalmers et al. (2021)`, a
perspective piece, not primary data), unlike pool swimming, where sweat
rates are low (`Cox et al. (2002)`). The posture is **measure, don't
guess**, not "drink less."

**Swimming is not running: immersion itself changes fluid regulation.**
`[ADAPTED: general-endurance]` Confidence: high for the diuresis itself.
Head-out immersion shifts ~700 mL of blood centrally, suppressing
vasopressin and driving diuresis and natriuresis (`Epstein (1992)`).
Practical consequence: **she urinates during the swim, so post-swim
body-mass loss overestimates sweat loss** — including in published swim
"sweat rate" figures derived from body mass. **`Coach judgment:`** the
further inference that post-exit vasopressin rebound explains her specific
low urine output is speculative and low-confidence — it is not established
evidence and must not be presented as such. **Test:** have her void
immediately before the post-swim weigh-in, so immersion-driven urine loss
isn't miscounted as a sweat deficit she then "replaces."

## Gaps

No swim-specific glycogen-depletion or duration-ceiling literature exists —
every timeline study cited above is cycling/running, male, and decades old.
Nothing addresses masters/older female athletes' substrate use specifically.
No study has measured the performance cost of feed stops in swimming, nor
the optimal carbohydrate concentration for a swim feed (the
concentration-vs-fluid-volume trade-off in this file has no data behind it).
EAH incidence in *warm-water* ultra swims specifically is unknown (Wagner is
a cool lake, Rogers a late-summer ocean swim). No field heuristic exists to
interpret low urine output + thirst short of a blood test — which is exactly
why the answer here is "get a serum sodium," not an invented rule. The
150%/sodium rehydration protocol has never been validated after an
ultra-distance swim specifically. Whether a "trained duration ceiling" can
be deliberately and durably raised, and over what timeframe, is untested;
`06-long-swim-progression.md`'s ladder is the current answer, and it rests
on coach judgment, not evidence.
